# **Executive Summary** 
The Product Playbook is a comprehensive approach to product management that offers significant benefits for organisations and employees. It encompasses the entire product development lifecycle, from idea creation to product success, and is guided by key principles and practices. 

## Benefits for Organizations 
Modern product management enhances an organisation's ability to create products that resonate with users. It improves resource allocation, fosters innovation, and reduces the risk of large-scale product failures. The approach promotes data-driven decision making, improves cross-functional collaboration, and often leads to increased customer satisfaction and loyalty. Financially, it can result in both improved value creation and lower costs. Organisations adopting these practices often become more attractive to high-quality professionals and are better positioned for long-term sustainability. 

## Benefits for Employees 
The model transforms the work experience across various roles. Product managers take on more holistic and strategic roles, while product owners gain clearer understanding and empowerment in decision-making. Software architects and lead engineers broaden their perspective beyond technical excellence to consider user experience and business value. Business stakeholders enjoy a more collaborative and transparent working environment. The model fosters continuous learning, cross-functional collaboration, and often leads to a stronger sense of purpose and job satisfaction. 

## Product Development Lifecycle 
The model comprises 4 main components. Although outlined here in distinct form, the components often interlink and happen in non-sequential ways. 

- **Product Discovery:** This initial stage involves generating and assessing ideas from various sources through research, user feedback, and feasibility studies. 
- **Product Strategy:** This stage involves defining the product's vision, goals, target audience, value proposition, key features, competitive positioning, and success metrics. 
- **Product Delivery:** This phase often follows agile methodologies, emphasising flexibility, continuous improvement, and close collaboration. 
- **Product Success:** This includes the launch and early adoption phase, scaling and growth, and maturity and continuous improvement. 

## [Product Discovery](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-discovery/) 
This critical process serves as the foundation for creating solutions that address user needs while aligning with business objectives. It involves collaborative efforts from diverse stakeholders and typically requires 20-30% of team capacity during each development cycle. The process includes identifying problems, gathering information, validating problems, and assessing business alignment and feasibility. 

## [Product Strategy](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-strategy/)
Product strategy serves as an overall outline for the more tactical steps within the model. It bridges the gap between high-level organisational vision and day-to-day tactical aspects of product development. Key elements include defining the target group, understanding user needs, considering user interface and experience, and aligning with business goals. Various frameworks exist to help teams create and articulate their product strategy. 

## [Product Delivery](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-delivery/)
Agile Product Delivery focuses on delivering valuable products and services to customers in a continuous and customer-centric manner. It involves prioritizing features based on their economic value and implementation time, developing on a fixed cadence, and releasing on demand. By building a continuous delivery pipeline with DevOps practices, organizations can automate the process of building, testing, and deploying software, enabling faster and more reliable delivery. The goal is to achieve business agility by delivering the right solutions to the right customers at the right time.

## [Product Success](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-success/)
Achieving product success requires continuous iteration, managing user engagement, and effective problem-solving. It involves measuring product performance, striving for product-market fit, navigating conflicts between corporate goals and user interests, and choosing appropriate user engagement models. Managing product complexity and preventing its accumulation are crucial for long-term success. 

## Working habits for product teams 
Merck's has decided on five core working habits for product teams:

- **User Centricity**: Focus on solving real user problems to boost business success and make a positive impact 
- **Autonomy in Execution**: Determine the best ways forward while aligning with the goals of the business and of other teams 
- **Responsibility and Accountability**: Feel empowered to make decisions and actively take ownership for the outcome of successes. 
- **Flexibility and Adaptability**: Adapt strategies and priorities in response to changes, leveraging learning cycles to enhance the team's agility and effectiveness. 
- **Ecosystem Mindset**: Maintain constant communication with other units to align with and adapt to system-wide changes. 

## Summary
The Product Playbook offers an Operating Model that is comprehensive approach that transforms how organisations develop and manage products. It offers significant benefits for both the organisation and its employees, fostering innovation, improving efficiency, and ultimately leading to more successful products. By following the product development lifecycle, emphasising product discovery and strategy, and adhering to key principles, organisations can position themselves for success in today's competitive business landscape.

# **Advantages of Modern Product Management**

Adopting modern product management practices offers numerous compelling advantages for organisations, positioning them for success in today's competitive business landscape.

## Enhanced User-Centric Approach
At its core, modern product management enhances an organisation's ability to create products and functionality that truly resonates with users. By emphasising a deep understanding of user needs and business demands, organisations can develop offerings that are more likely to succeed. This user-centric approach, coupled with agile methodologies and iterative development processes, allows organisations to bring better products to market faster and more efficiently.

## Improved Resource Allocation
One of the key benefits is improved resource allocation. Modern product management practices enable organisations to prioritise features and initiatives based on user needs and business value. This ensures that development resources are invested in areas that will yield the greatest returns, minimising waste and maximising impact. It will generally lead to fewer products, but each product will come with a strong value proposition.

## Fostering Innovation
Innovation is another significant advantage. The structured approach to idea validation and product development inherent in modern product management can foster a culture of innovation within the organisation. This helps organisations to stay ahead of challenges and competitors and continually offers fresh, valuable solutions to their users.

## Enhanced Risk Management
Risk management is substantially improved through these practices. Continuous validation and iterative development allow organisations to identify and address issues early in the product life cycle. This approach significantly reduces the risk of large-scale product failures, which can be costly both financially and in terms of user reputation.

## Increased Customer Satisfaction
Customer satisfaction typically sees a marked increase with the adoption of modern product management. The constant focus on user needs and the practice of continually improving based on feedback leads to products that better meet customer expectations. This, in turn, can lead to increased customer loyalty and positive word-of-mouth, both crucial for long-term success.

## Data-Driven Decision Making
From a strategic perspective, modern product management promotes data-driven decision making. The emphasis on metrics and analytics enables more informed and objective choices about product direction and resource allocation. This data-centric approach can lead to better strategic decisions and improved overall business performance.

## Improved Cross-Functional Collaboration
Cross-functional collaboration within the organisation often improves as well. Strong product teams serve as bridges between different parts of the organisation, fostering better communication and alignment between departments. This enhanced collaboration can lead to more cohesive product development efforts and a more unified organisational strategy.

## Financial Benefits
Financially, the benefits can be substantial. By creating fewer products that better meet user needs, organisations often see both improved value creation as well as lower costs. The more efficient use of resources can lead to cost savings, while high user satisfaction can result in increased product usage and value creation. All these factors contribute to improved overall financial performance.

## Talent Attraction and Retention
In terms of talent management, organisations known for strong product management practices often become more attractive to high-quality professionals. This can give organisations an edge in the competitive market for top talent, both in attracting new employees and retaining existing ones.

## Long-Term Sustainability
Perhaps most importantly, adopting modern product management practices contributes significantly to an organisation's long-term sustainability. By consistently delivering value to customers and swiftly adapting to changes, organisations can ensure their continued relevance and growth.

## Conclusion
Done well, modern product management can create a virtuous cycle for organisations. Better products lead to more satisfied customers, driving business success. This success, in turn, allows for further investment in product development, fuelling continued growth and innovation and creating better products, which starts the cycle again.

## **What does working in the Product Operating Model mean for me?**
The adoption of a product operating model significantly transforms the work experience for employees across various roles, particularly those directly involved in product development and management.

### Product Managers
For product managers, this model represents **a shift towards a more holistic and strategic role**. They become central figures in product development, responsible for aligning business objectives with user needs and technological possibilities. This expanded responsibility often leads to increased job satisfaction as product managers can see the direct impact of their decisions on both user experience and business outcomes. They develop a broader skill set, becoming adept at everything from user research and data analysis to strategic planning and cross-functional leadership.

### Product Owners
Product owners, working closely with product managers, find themselves more empowered in their day-to-day decision-making. They gain a clearer understanding of the product vision and can more effectively prioritise backlog items. This clarity often results in **more meaningful work** as they can directly connect their daily tasks to larger product and business goals.

### Architects and Senior Engineers
Software architects and lead engineers experience a shift in their approach to technical decisions. In a product-centric model, they're not just focused on technical excellence but also on how technical choices impact user experience and business value. This broader perspective can be both challenging and rewarding, pushing them to **think beyond code and consider the wider implications of their work**. They often find themselves more involved in early-stage product discussions, contributing their technical expertise to shape product direction from the outset.

### Business Stakeholders
For closely aligned business stakeholders, the product operating model often means **a more collaborative and transparent working environment**. They gain better visibility into the product development process and have more opportunities to provide input and feedback. This increased involvement can lead to a stronger sense of ownership and alignment with product goals.

### For all roles
Across all these roles, the product operating model fosters a culture of continuous learning and adaptability. Employees are encouraged to stay abreast of market trends, user needs, and technological advancements. This constant evolution can be **intellectually stimulating** and helps keep skills relevant in a rapidly changing job market. The model also tends to break down traditional silos, encouraging **more cross-functional collaboration**. Employees across different roles find themselves working more closely together, gaining exposure to different perspectives and skill sets. This collaborative environment can lead to more innovative solutions and a more engaging work experience.

However, it's important to note that **this model also comes with challenges**. The increased responsibility and need for broader skills can be stressful for some employees. The fast-paced, iterative nature of product development can also lead to pressure to deliver quickly and constantly adapt to changes.

Despite these challenges, most employees find the product operating model **ultimately rewarding**. It offers greater autonomy, the opportunity to have a more significant impact, and a clearer connection between their work and user value. For many, this leads to a stronger sense of purpose and pride in their work.

# **Product Development Lifecycle**
Developing digital products covers a comprehensive arc that guides a product from its initial conception to its successful delivery and beyond. This lifecycle encompasses several key stages, each important for creating a product that resonates with users and drives business growth. Different teams and individuals will contribute their work to various stages of the product life cycle.

## Idea Creation and Validation (-> [Product Discovery](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-discovery/))
The product life cycle begins with idea generation. Ideas can come from various sources: market research, strategic directives, user/customer feedback, technological advancements, or internal innovation initiatives. Teams can also make conscious efforts to generate ideas through user research. However, not all ideas are viable or valuable. One of the important tasks of a product team is to validate these ideas. Idea validation involves assessing the potential of a product concept before significant resources are invested.

This stage is referred to as Product Discovery and contains the following main activities:
- **Establish Context:** Understanding the environment, business needs and existing solutions.
- **User Interviews:** Gathering insights directly from (potential) users about their needs and pain points.
- **Problem-Solution Fit Analysis:** Ensuring that the proposed product solves a real and significant problem for users and that it fits into the general business strategy.
**- Feasibility Studies:** Assessing whether the product can be developed with available resources and technology.

The goal of this stage is to separate promising ideas from those that are unlikely to succeed, reducing the risk of investing in products that won't resonate with users or align with business objectives.

## Creating a [Product Strategy](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-strategy/)
Once ideas have been validated and it has been established that there is value creation, the next step is to develop an initial product strategy. This strategy will be based of results of the Product Discovery activities and exteral strategic factors such as business goals. A strategy can be turned into a roadmap later, guiding future decisions and actions related to the product. Key components of a Product Strategy include:

- **Vision and Goals:** Defining what the product aims to achieve in the long term and how it aligns with overall business objectives.
- **Target Audience:** Clearly identifying who the product is for, including user personas and user segments and types.
- **Value Proposition:** Articulating how the product will solve user problems or fulfil needs better than existing solutions.
- **Key Features and Functionality:** Outlining the 3-5 core capabilities that will deliver the promised value.
- **Competitive Positioning:** Deciding how the product will differentiate itself and how users will learn about it.
- **Success Metrics:** Establishing key performance indicators (KPIs) to measure the product's success.

The product strategy should be flexible enough to adapt to changing market conditions or new insights, while still providing a clear direction for the product's development.

## Product Development and Agile Delivery (-> [Product Delivery](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-delivery/))
With a product strategy in place, the focus shifts to operational product development. In modern product management, this phase often follows agile methodologies, which prioritise flexibility, continuous improvement, and close collaboration between development teams and stakeholders.

Key aspects of agile product development include:
- **Product Roadmap:** A mid-term outlook on what functionality will be developed
- **Product Backlog:** Creating and maintaining a prioritised list of features, improvements, and fixes.
- **Sprint Planning:** Breaking down development work into short cycles (usually 1-4 weeks) with specific goals.
- **Daily Stand-ups:** Facilitating regular team communication to track progress and address obstacles.
- **Sprint Reviews and Retrospectives:** Evaluating completed work and continuously improving processes.

Throughout this phase, the product team must ensure that development efforts remain aligned with the product strategy and user needs. This involves constant prioritisation, making trade-offs, and communicating with stakeholders.

## Achieving [Product Success](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-success/)
The development stage will result in a product (often not a full product, but new functionality in an existing product) that can be shown to users. Depending on the maturity there are different approaches on how to proceed.

### Launch and Early Adoption
An initial product (or functionality) launch marks a critical transition from development to market presence. Teams should think of the following aspects:

- **Go-to-Market Strategy:** Planning how the product will be introduced to its target audience.
- **User Onboarding:** Ensuring new users can quickly understand and derive value from the product.
- **Support Infrastructure:** Setting up channels for user feedback and assistance.
- **Metrics Tracking:** Implementing systems to monitor key performance indicators.

The focus during this phase is on driving early adoption and gathering real-world user feedback. This often involves closely monitoring user behaviour, addressing initial bugs or usability issues, and iterating quickly based on user responses.

### Scaling and Growth
As a product or a new functionality gains traction, the focus shifts to scaling and growth. This phase involves:

- **Feature Optimization:** Optimising capabilities based on user feedback.
- **Performance Optimization:** Ensuring the product can handle increased usage and data volumes.
- **User Retention:** Implementing strategies to keep existing users engaged and satisfied.
- **Expansion:** Exploring new user segments or use cases that can be served.

During this phase, data-driven decision making becomes increasingly important. Product teams must analyse user metrics, market trends, and competitive landscapes to guide the product's evolution.

### Maturity and Continuous Improvement
Over time, products and functionality will reach maturity. Most improvements have been done, the focus now shifts to optimization and efficiency. Key activities in this phase include:

- **Feature Refinement:** Streamlining existing features based on long-term usage data.
- **Operational Efficiency:** Optimising processes to reduce costs and improve reliability.
- **Legacy Management:** Balancing the needs of long-term users with the desire for innovation.
- **Decommissioning:** Deciding and managing end-of-life scenarios.

Although they have reached a mature state, products and functionality will always require ongoing attention and improvement to remain relevant and valuable to users as well as viable to run and support from a technical perspective.

There are five core working habits for product teams at Merck.

**User Centricity** is fundamental to building successful products, placing user needs, preferences, and experiences at the heart of all product decisions and development processes. This principle focuses on solving real user problems and meeting genuine needs, rather than simply implementing user requests or following strategic directives. It implies research, empathy, and ongoing product feedback, utilising tools such as user personas, user journeys, and UX design and testing. The goal is to increase user satisfaction, loyalty, and adoption rates while balancing user needs with business objectives. 

**Autonomy in Execution** is designed to empower teams and team members to make decisions and take initiative while aligning with the overall business objectives. This principle encourages proactive problem-solving and allows for boundary-spanning behaviour, where team members go beyond their traditional role boundaries. It promotes data-driven decision making, providing teams with the tools and information needed to make informed choices. **Responsibility and Accountability** is a core to being successful. This principle implies a deep-rooted sense of ownership over the product's success, covering the impact on users, the business, and the broader market. Teams set clear, measurable, and ambitious goals, with all members personally invested in the outcomes. This approach emphasises taking responsibility for both successes and setbacks, conducting retrospectives to derive learnings for future initiatives. Transparency in data-driven decision-making processes and outcomes is paramount, fostering trust and alignment across the organisation. 

**Flexibility and Adaptability** is another key tenet, embodied in the mantra of "strong opinions, loosely held." This approach encourages forming well-reasoned viewpoints while remaining open to new information and willing to adapt. **Ecosystem Mindset** considers the long-term health of the product and its broader ecosystem. This perspective extends influence beyond the immediate product team to the wider organisation, promoting cross-functional collaboration and breaking down silos between different departments. It encourages sharing objectives and KPIs across teams, fostering innovation through the mixing of different viewpoints and expertise. It also puts a high value on sharing current priorities and plans across teams to ensure the organisation is aware of the bigger context.

# Product Discovery
# **Summary**

Product Discovery is a critical process in product development, serving as the foundation for creating solutions that genuinely address user needs while aligning with business objectives. This iterative approach allows product teams to identify real user problems, explore potential solutions, and validate their ideas with users and key stakeholders before committing significant resources to development. The process closely aligns with the first diamond in the double-diamond design model, emphasising the importance of thoroughly understanding the problem space before moving into solution development. 

 One of the key strengths of Product Discovery lies in its collaborative nature. It brings together a diverse group of stakeholders, each contributing unique perspectives and expertise. Product Managers or Product Owners often lead the process, but they are not the only participants. User Research and Design professionals often play important roles in gathering and analysing user data, translating insights into tangible designs and building prototypes. Engineers contribute by assessing technical feasibility and helping to build prototypes, while data analysts can provide quantitative insights to inform decision-making. External stakeholders, including customers, business leaders, and enterprise architects, also provide valuable input throughout the process. 

Product Discovery and Product Delivery are closely intertwined, with Discovery ideally staying one development cycle ahead of Delivery. This forward-looking approach allows teams to have validated solution designs ready for the next development cycle. To achieve this, teams typically allocate about 20-30% of their capacity to Discovery activities, even during active development phases. While this may seem counterintuitive, especially when facing tight deadlines, this ensures that teams are building the right solutions rather than just building quickly. 

The Discovery process itself involves several key stages. It begins with identifying problems and business opportunities, which requires a clear understanding of the available scope for new developments. Teams then normally conduct information gathering, using various techniques such as stakeholder interviews, surveys, and interactive sessions. The next step is problem validation, where teams confirm which identified issues can create significant value when addressed. This stage often involves hypothesis testing and gathering both quantitative and qualitative evidence. 

Once potential solutions are identified, teams must assess their business alignment, ensuring that proposed ideas fit with the overall company strategy. This is followed by a feasibility and viability check, where technical and commercial considerations come into play. Throughout this process, teams must carefully manage stakeholder expectations and input, particularly from influential stakeholders who may have outsized influence on decision-making. 

Measuring the effectiveness of Product Discovery is crucial for continuous improvement. Teams can track metrics such as the quality and quantity of feedback received, the rate of hypothesis validation, and the time taken to validate ideas. The conversion rate from discovery to development and user engagement with prototypes are also valuable indicators. Conversely, signs of ineffective discovery include inconclusive results, a high number of unvalidated assumptions moving to development, frequent pivots during development, low product adoption, and consistently negative user feedback. 

Best practices for Product Discovery include maintaining control over the stakeholder input process, adhering firmly to the overall product strategy, and learning to say no to promising ideas that don't align with core objectives. This last point is particularly challenging but a key requirement for building a cohesive product. Teams must develop the skill to select the right subset of ideas from a larger pool of seemingly good options. 

Finally, documentation plays a vital role in Product Discovery, helping teams avoid repeating past mistakes and providing guidance for future efforts. This can include records of discarded ideas and the reasoning behind those decisions, instructions for interpreting product analytics, and guidelines for gathering and validating information. 

In conclusion, Product Discovery is a complex but essential process. When done effectively, it ensures that product teams focus their efforts on solving real, valuable problems, creating solutions that provide real value to users while also meeting business objectives.

# Who is involved in Product Discovery?
Here is an overview of the various teams and functions and how they should be involved. As always with empowered teams, responsibilities can shift between functions, especially if specific functions are not explicitly staffed. For example, it is common that Product Owners/Managers take over responsibilities from the UX or the Data Analysis domains.

Contrary to widely held belief, Product Discovery is not the sole responsibility of someone that has the word "product" in their job title. Roles like Product Manager or Product Owner are often deeply involved in Product Discovery, but they are by no means the only participants.

## Inside the empowered team

### Product
The product function is at the heart of Product Discovery. Its main purpose is to facilitate activities and translate the results into a cohesive picture. They interpret and translate the overall vision and strategy, facilitate the discovery process, and ensure alignment with business goals. Members of the product function also lead the coordination between teams, speak with users and stakeholders, validate problems and opportunities, and manage the discovery timeline.

### User Research and Design (UX/UI)
The UX function is responsible for creating insights from external stakeholders and turning these into visual and tangible product functionality. They gather and analyse data about user behaviour and needs. They conduct user interviews, surveys, and usability tests, synthesising insights to guide the discovery process. They translate user needs and feedback into tangible designs and prototypes and they create and iterate on prototypes based on user interactions and feedback sessions.

### Engineering
Members of the Engineering function are critical to the validation phase but ideally involved earlier to assess feasibility and viability of potential solutions. They shadow and understand results from user research and often provide insights into technical feasibility and help build prototypes. They can participate in discussions about design and functionality, offering a realistic perspective on what can be developed efficiently.

### Data Analysis
Data Analysts provide the expertise needed to turn raw data into meaningful information, especially when it comes to quantitative data. They drive implementation of product analytics and analyse data from various sources to inform decision-making and validate assumptions. They provide quantitative insights into user behaviours and prototype performance, helping to measure the impact of tested features.

## External stakeholders (Outside the empowered team)

### Customers/Users
Users are the #1 external stakeholders that teams should focus on during Product Discovery. They can give feedback directly applicable to their everyday usage. Users are the ultimate source of information on what needs and problems can and should be addressed. They participate in interviews, testing sessions, and provide ongoing feedback to ensure that the development aligns with real-world use.

### Business Stakeholders
Different from individual users, business stakeholders often focus on higher-level goals and problems that may or may not align with individual user needs. They give feedback on business realities, financial expectations, and business strategy and offer strategic insights during discovery. They can also help define product positioning, and ensure that product solutions meet business objectives.

### Enterprise Architecture
Enterprise Architects focus on the overall technical architecture for the wider company and lobby for solutions that keep the architecture cohesive. They give feedback on technical feasibility and viability of individual solutions and offer strategic insights on technology and upcoming innovations to avoid incompatible and/or island solutions.

### Group Leadership
Leadership plays a pivotal role in Product Discovery. They need to provide the overall guidance and guard rails that apply to the team by outlining the overall strategy, explaining priorities, reviewing intermediate results and creating alignment across multiple teams.

# User Centricity and Product Discovery
User centricity is a design and development approach that prioritises the needs, preferences, and experiences of the end-users of a product or service.

It involves understanding users' goals, behaviours, and pain points through methods such as user research, feedback, and usability testing. The core idea is to create solutions that are intuitive, accessible, and tailored to the users' real-world contexts, thereby enhancing satisfaction and engagement. User centricity emphasises empathy and a deep commitment to delivering value from the user's perspective.

That said, user centricity is NOT about building exactly what users ask for.

The difference between building products based on what individual users "say" and what most users really "need" lies in understanding the underlying motivations and problems that drive individual user requests. When users say what they *want*, they often describe surface-level features or functionalities based on their current understanding and recent experiences. Users often articulate their desires in terms of solutions they envision, but these might not address the root cause of their issues effectively - and they may actively make the life of other users worse.

Product creators need to translate the expressed desires from individual users into a deeper understanding of the underlying needs, which is often complex and nuanced. When this id not done, products often acquire conflicting, overlapping and mismatched functionality and will struggle to solve large, real problems in a long-term setting.

The main goal of a product team during Product Discovery is to "discover" the real problems. This is deeply user-centric, but to do this, the team needs to go beyond the short-term, specific, and "right now" nature of individual user feedback.

# Relationship between Discovery and Delivery
Although the sections are separate and the double-diamond model implies only a minor touchpoint, Product Discovery and Product Delivery are co-dependent on each other. There is also a crucial time dimension that teams need to keep in mind.

In general, Product Discovery should be "ahead" of Product Delivery by one development cycle. Assuming a quarterly or 12-week delivery cycle (as is common in SAFe and other frameworks), Product Discovery activities in the current cycle should aim to create validated problem statements to be handled by Product Delivery in the following cycle. For this to work, each team should reserve a certain amount of capacity during each development cycle to conduct the necessary discovery activities for the next cycle. The time commitment for Discovery will differ by team member, but a good rule of thumb is to reserve roughly 20-30% of the total team's time for Discovery (more for product/design, less for engineering).

Taking dive out of delivery capacity can feel counterintuitive for teams because it takes away time from delivering solutions and dealing with problems of the present. Teams often feel a lot of pressure to use less time for any activities that are not strictly related to Product Delivery, especially in times of strict deadlines and important launches. It is critically important though to not forget about Discovery activities (and Product Strategy work) - even in busy periods. As eloquently stated by Melissa Perri in her book "The Build Trap", underinvesting in Product Discovery often causes an adverse effect: Teams rush into the development of functionality which may not solve real problems. Phrased more dramatically: Allocating too much capacity to Delivery may make a team *run faster*, but there is a high likelihood that it will *run in the wrong direction*.

# Identify problems & business opportunities
Product Discovery acts as the gatekeeper to the development phase and equals the first diamond in the double-diamond model. Following this model, identifying problems equals the Discover phase in the first diamond of that framework. The goal of this phase is to gather as much information as possible/necessary about problems and opportunities that exist, why they exist, who they affect and what the implication of each problem is.

## Understand the Available Scope
The above states "as much information as possible/necessary". Before starting to gather input, an important question needs to be answered: How much room for "new" things does the team have in the next delivery cycle? Depending on the answer, different approaches should be taken.

1. **Green field:** In a green field approach the team will not have any existing commitments and can freely decide on how to spend their time in the next delivery cycle. This is often the case in early-stage startups or newly incubated teams in larger companies. In a case like this, teams can cast the net wide and do extensive problem identification.

2. **Evolution:** In an evolution approach the team has an existing solution and is about to finish work on their current set of tasks and ideas. They are looking for new ideas on how to continue and improve their solution. In a case like this, the team should spend a fair amount of time on identifying problems and opportunities. It is widespread practice to start with a focus on existing users and stakeholders, but the team can also spend time to research potential topics outside their current scope.

3. **Fine-tuning:** In the fine-tuning approach the team is committed to develop a specific functionality but will have some room to make changes or amendments to what they are doing. This is most often the case for established products. In a case like this, the team should target their existing user-base to make sure that they are addressing the problems of their users to the best extent possible.

4. **Heads-down:** In the heads-down approach a team is fully immersed in the development process. This often takes place when a team is close to launching a new product or feature. In a case like this, identifying new problems and opportunities for the next cycle should be kept to a minimum since any input is likely not going to be considered. To avoid disappointment of users and stakeholders, it is better to communicate the situation openly and "re-open" the gathering of information at a later stage.

Larger teams can work on multiple products at the same time, so multiple distinct phases can coincide. It is also possible that while a part of the team is still in the heads-down phase, some team members are already switching to an evolution phase. In this case it is important to clearly communicate to stakeholders for which phase and topic requirements are being collected to avoid disappointment.

## Conduct information gathering
Once it is clear what (and how much) information is needed, actual information gathering can start. Here is a checklist for teams.

1. **Stakeholder Identification:** The first step is to identify the relevant parties to ask for input. This should most likely include the primary users of the product in question and those who will be impacted by its deployment. This should obviously target end users from each department but typically also includes business and technical stakeholders such as department heads, IT personnel, enablers, or coaches.

   You should also ensure that stakeholders from various levels (executive, managerial, operational) are included to gather a comprehensive set of needs and expectations.

2. **Special case: End users in large companies, B2B2C or hub-hub-spoke models**: In many cases, a product team ends up being "removed" from its end customers, which frequently happens once there are organisations (either internal or external) that handle most of the end customer contacts.

   This is common in large companies that have customer support departments or large sales teams. It can also happen when a company mainly sells to wholesalers, consultancies, or aggregators. It also tends to be common for internal platform products on topics such as CRM, Analytics or Finance, especially when these are used across multiple departments or subsidiaries.

   There are two specific approaches that should be taken:

   - Make an effort to speak with end users: Nothing beats speaking to an actual user. While it can cause some additional work, it is the only way that a product team can really understand how users think about, feel, and use the product.

   ![image name](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/docs-assets/pom/talk_to_the_user.PNG)

   - Utilise (and train) the intermediaries: There is a lot of value in having a team or organisation in between the product team and the end customer. Such groups act as natural filters and through the nature of their work (hopefully) develop a deep understanding on what "their" users do and want. What they may not know is how to make use of this knowledge and feed it to product teams to improve the product. This can be achieved by training such organisations to become valuable channels of condensed and prioritised user feedback.

3. **Choosing Information Gathering Techniques:** Based on the needs, various techniques can be used. The team can observe end users or use one-on-one interviews with key stakeholders to understand specific needs, pain points with current systems, and expectations from the new product. Surveys and questionnaires provide a way to get input from a larger audience to gather a broad perspective and quantitative data about needs and usage patterns. In more complex cases, interactive sessions can bring multiple stakeholders together. The team can use techniques like brainstorming, SWOT analysis, and prototype discussions to foster collaboration and consensus.

4. **Qualification and Quantification:** Regardless of the technique used, it is critically important to understand the importance of each of the information received. Where possible, this should be done through quantifying questions ("% of survey respondents stated issue A had a large impact on their daily work"). Often, the information will come in via anecdotal evidence or qualitative data though. In this case, special attention must be paid to document the qualitative nature of information.

5. **Stakeholder Expectation Setting:** Last but not least stakeholders should understand what they are giving input on and how it will be used. It makes a difference if someone is asked for ideas for a far-away future or about choosing one of three options how their daily work will change next month.

   You should also ensure that stakeholders from various levels (executive, managerial, operational) are included to gather a comprehensive set of needs and expectations.

# Validate problems
The outcome of the Discover phase from the double-diamond model usually ends with a large set of things that were identified as problems and opportunities. If it was done well, each problem and opportunity is well documented and has relevant contextual information attached to it.

The next step is to confirm which of the identified issues can create significant value when addressed. The goal is to reduce the number of issues to a meaningful and manageable amount, either by removing issues or by combining multiple issues into a bigger issue that can be addressed in one go. This is the Define stage of the first diamond or the validation stage.

Compared with identifying problems, which relies on exploratory methods, validation uses confirmatory methods like hypothesis testing, surveys, and usability testing to gather quantitative and qualitative evidence. In general, teams will use hypothesis testing for this. Based on their knowledge of issues they will state a hypothesis and then look for evidence to support this hypothesis. At the end of problem validation, the list of "all problems" should be reduced to the list of "important problems". A good framework for validation follows below.

Sometimes it can be helpful to think in the solution space during problem validation, but this is not the main goal. Using tools like prototypes can help better understand problems and assess their importance but they should be used as temporary tools rather than actual solution sketches.

**NOTE**: While the below looks like a linear process, the included activities can be (and are!) done in different orders. Teams can combine, re-order or repeat individual steps based on the company environment, skill sets and availability of team members and what is most useful to them on a day-to-day basis. The only important piece is that all of them should be done at some point.

## 1) Gather evidence
The first step is to find evidence on which identified problems or opportunities will most likely create a lot of value.

The best way to approach this step is to change the nature of the information from "descriptive input" to "evidence". Descriptive input is the information given to the team in its original form. As an example, let's take a user statement like "it takes me way too long to request access to this dataset". This is likely an honest statement, but it is not clear yet what the impact of solving this issue would be. To assess this, the team must assess things like "how many users are affected", "what will happen if these users get fast access", etc.

To create evidence, the team should look for supportive data of each statement. For some of these questions product analytics tools like Mixpanel or Amplitude can give quantitative answers. For others it may be necessary to go back to the business as a whole or a specific information source and ask clarifying questions. It can also be helpful to look at external sources (reports, web articles, forum discussions, ...).

**Tool recommendation: Confidence meter**


<img src=" https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/docs-assets/pom/confidence_meter.PNG " width="700"/>

Source: https://itamargilad.com/the-tool-that-will-help-you-choose-better-product-ideas/

## 2) Assess Business Alignment
Once evidence around specific ideas or inputs has been created and the impact of each of these has been assessed the team should have a better understanding which user problem should be solved to create value. In other words: Evidence helps to confirm the fact that something will be beneficial to someone or even many.

What evidence can't do is give any indication of how impactful this something will be to the business as a whole. The next step is to cross-reference the list of potential improvements with the existing strategy. To do this, teams need to understand the overall business strategy and their overall goal of delivering value. Only with that understanding they can interpret evidence further.

The reality is that most teams are constrained and need to make choices. Topics that have low priority from a strategic perspective should be marked as such and set aside - even if there is evidence and impact to support the value of a potential change. To stay with the example, if the overall strategy explicitly states that "speed" is not important when it comes to accessing datasets then this input probably shouldn't result in a product improvement. This is also incredibly important if multiple teams work on related topics - they should ideally prioritise similar strategic topics.

## 3) Check for feasibility and viability
At this point, the team should have a set of items that fulfil three criteria: They solve real user problems, there is evidence they will have business impact and they align with the general strategy. All these criteria indicate that the item is desired by the customer. All items on the list would be happily received as product improvements from both individual users as well as business stakeholders.

At this point, the process changes course. Until this point, the discussion has been mostly driven by the Product and Design members of the product team - now comes the point where these items need to be challenged regarding their technical feasibility and commercial viability. Not all desirable things - teleportation(!) - are technically feasible or commercially viable.

This task generally sits with the more technical members of a product team such as architects, software engineers or a DevOps liaison - but product and design members should be involved as well.

Topics that should be looked at in this activity are:

- **Cost of Development:** Be realistic about the overall effort required to develop, launch, and operate the functionality and compare it with the expected impact.
- **Time horizon:** Ensure that the time available to provide the functionality matches the time that is needed to provide it.
- **Integration and Compatibility:** Understand the existing IT infrastructure, software, and data sources to ensure the new product integrates smoothly without disrupting existing systems.
- **Data Governance and Security:** Be aware of the company's data governance policies and security requirements, especially for handling sensitive or proprietary data.
- **Scalability:** Consider future needs and scalability. The system should be able to handle increasing data volumes or changing analytical demands.

# Handing over to Delivery
The very last step in Product Discovery is to hand over a list of validated problems or opportunities to the Product Delivery. Different teams differ in their handling of this task.

Example: In some cases, visual prototypes may have been used during the validation stage. If and how these are transitioned to Delivery is something that differs between teams. It depends on things like how many design resources are available where and how successful the prototypes were.

There is also a difference between initial Discovery and ongoing Discovery. While initial Discovery (often used in green field stages) can have more of a formal handover process, continuous discovery generally involves more back and forth.

How handovers are handled also depends a lot on how big the overlap of participants is between Discovery and Delivery. For example, if engineers were deeply involved in Discovery, they may opt to document the resulting user stories themselves they may also take ownership of delivering parts of the functionality end-to-end. In cases where engineering is further removed, they may require a product person to "translate" the results of the Discovery process into an artefact that they will use during Product Delivery.

## Document your findings
A common issue that product teams deal with is how to document their findings. Discovery is somewhat of an ephemeral process, the "final" result gets created during Product Delivery and many findings are discarded along the way. This can lead to teams not documenting their findings in a way that future efforts don't have to restart.

Examples of documentation:
- Discarded "problems" and the reasons
- Instructions on how to read Product Analytics
- Guidelines for gathering information the right way
- List of validation criteria that should be applied
- Lists of valuable sources of feedback and known red herrings
- Historic solution prototypes and their outcomes/results

## Process at Merck
The process of handing over results from Product Discovery to Product Delivery at Merck uses the following steps. Full details can be found at [Connecting Strategy](link) in the Product Delivery section.
1. Portfolio strategy supports enterprise business objectives and is connected to execution through strategic initiatives.
2. Portfolio roadmaps guide the organization in realizing strategic initiatives, typically spanning 1-3+ years.
3. Epics are containers for significant development initiatives, either at the ART (Agile Release Train) or Portfolio level.
4. Epics require a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) and approval by Portfolio Management.
5. The Epic Hypothesis Statement fulfills the vision and includes value statements, business outcomes, leading indicators, and non-functional requirements.
6. SAFe Lean startup strategy recommends an iterative build-measure-learn cycle for product innovation.
7. The Portfolio Kanban system visualizes the flow of epics from idea to implementation, with stages including Funnel, Reviewing, Analyzing, Ready, Implementing, and Done.
8. Epic estimation often uses T-shirt sizing in early stages, while Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF) is used for prioritization.
9. Two key portfolio events are:
   - Portfolio Sync: Monthly operational focus on portfolio progress
   - Strategic Portfolio Review: Quarterly review to optimize portfolio solutions

# Handling influential stakeholders
A key issue for many teams (especially internal teams and in B2B companies) is the existence of influential stakeholders. This can either be high-ranking executives, financial sponsors, anchor customers, department leads or other stakeholders that have some sort of leverage over the course of the product. The reason these stakeholders can be complicated to handle is that they often expect special treatment - and not always deserve it.

The most common example is when it comes to low-level requests. It is unquestioned that influential stakeholders should be heard when it comes to the business alignment stage of Product Discovery. They own the overall strategy, and their input is important here. The problem starts when they start to get involved in other stages, especially when they try to personally get involved in the problem gathering phase. For those topics, they rarely deserve any special status.

As with most things in life, not everyone can be treated the same way. As a simple model, influential stakeholders can be divided into four quadrants defined by the axis **expertise** and **alignment**.

1. **VIP:** VIPs are important allies and often of immense value. They are *both knowledgeable and aligned*. They often have extensive knowledge of the technology, the user problems, the business, or any other aspect that is relevant to the product, they know and respect the process and they are fully aligned on the overall direction. As a result, they *should* be treated as strong allies and given preferential treatment when it comes to all phases of collecting and validating feedback.

2. **Supporters:** Supporters are *aligned on the overall direction but lack expertise*. They want to be strong collaborators but they may need help and education on how to best contribute. They want the product to go into the same direction as the product team, but they may not understand what is technically feasible or how things really work "on-the-ground". This may cause many of their well-meant contributions to not be viable and creates the danger that they burn out once they feel their contributions are not taken into account properly. These stakeholder should be educated and turned into VIPs if possible.

3. **Eminences:** Eminences have a *lot of expertise, but don't agree with the direction*. These are often long-standing members of the organisation. They need to be carefully managed since their input is generally accurate, valuable and highly respected across the organisation - but when they do not align with the overall strategy and priority there is a real danger that their input derails the work of the team. As they try to influence towards their own perception of what is importance, they may give input in a way that contradicts what the team is trying to achieve - which is often difficult to handle due to their expertise and reputation. Teams must make an effort to align with these stakeholders - be it by convincing them of their own strategy or by adjusting the strategy in a way that these stakeholders can become VIPs eventually.

4. **Toxic:** The last category of stakeholders is the most dangerous one. They are *not aligned strategically AND do not understand the topic in depth*. They often use hierarchy and power dynamics to assert their influence. If they are able to force their opinions on a team it is nearly impossible for the team to be successful. The best way to approach this situation is to ask for help from VIP or Supporters to shift a toxic stakeholder towards a Supporter, after which the team can start educating them on any details. It is also important to note that even *toxic* stakeholders often have good intentions.

It is not always possible to shift stakeholders from one type to another, but over time teams have options to improve their relationships with stakeholders. In general, a good tactic for product teams is to first shift all non-aligned stakeholders to aligned and then educate all non-knowledgeable stakeholders to knowledgeable.

# Measuring Product Discovery
Measuring the effectiveness of Product Discovery involves evaluating both the process and the outcomes. Effective product discovery should lead to a deeper understanding of user needs and should result in the development of products that successfully meet these needs. Here are some key metrics and indicators to consider:

## Indicators of Effective Product Discovery
The following is an (incomplete) list of metrics that can be used to judge if the Product Discovery process works well and delivers results.

1. **Feedback Quality and Quantity:**  
   Is the team receiving actionable, clear feedback that can guide further iterations? The quality and volume of feedback can signal the effectiveness of discovery sessions.

2. **Hypothesis Validation Rate:**  
   How many hypotheses about user needs and behaviours are confirmed through testing? A higher rate of validated hypotheses indicates a good understanding of your user base.

3. **Time to Validate Ideas:**  
   How quickly is the team able to move from ideation to validated learning? Efficient cycles suggest a streamlined and effective discovery process.

4. **User Engagement with Prototypes:**  
   How actively are users interacting with prototypes or solution sketches? High engagement levels typically indicate that solution sketches are used in a way that resonates well with users and creates valuable user feedback.

5. **Conversion from Discovery to Development:**  
   What percentage of validated features move into the development phase? A higher conversion rate suggests that the discovery phase is producing the right number of viable, valuable product ideas.

## Indicators of Ineffective Product Discovery
Poor performance of the indicators above imply that your Product Discovery could be better, but there are other indicators that you should look out for.

1. **Inconclusive results:**  
   If your discovery process is elongated and frequently does not produce conclusive results, you might not apply a suitable set of methods and tools. It can also indicate other inefficiencies or difficulties in validating product concepts effectively.

2. **High Number of Unvalidated Assumptions Moving to Development:**  
   Starting development on features that have not gone through any validation processes indicates an immature Product Discovery phase and can lead to wasted resources and products that fail to meet market needs.

3. **Frequent Pivots during Development:**  
   If there's a need for frequent significant changes to your products during development or after launch, it may indicate poor initial understanding or validation of user needs during discovery.

4. **Low Product Adoption:**  
   While not available during the discovery phase itself, this can be analysed retroactively. If upon launch, the adoption rates are significantly lower than projections, it suggests that the product discovery phase may not have accurately identified or validated user needs.

5. **Consistently Negative User Feedback:**  
   Not all solutions will receive positive feedback, but consistently negative feedback or a lack of enthusiasm in user testing sessions can signal a more fundamental misalignment between the general product concept and user expectations or needs.

# Best practices

## Control the stakeholder input process
A big reason for being successful is to control the process of capturing stakeholder input. Teams relevant for this can range from Legal to Customer Support and Inside Sales to Compliance. There are three best practices.

1. **Stay in Control:** To solicit high-quality input from other teams it is important that the product team controls the cross-functional setup. They know best when they need information and what information they need.

2. **Provide a Framework:** Most teams are not used to working with product teams, so they will need help. Product teams should be clear on the expectations toward other teams regarding how their expertise is helpful. For example, it should be clear to all cross-functional stakeholders what information "feature requests" should include.

3. **Cultivate Liaisons:** In every team there will be some team members who have an affinity towards building products. Ideally a product team builds strong relationships with these individuals and helps them to become familiar with the inner workings of the product team. These liaisons can become advocates and important multipliers inside the cross-functional teams.

## Be Firm on the Overall Strategy
In most companies, various forces pull on empowered product teams. Sales needs features. Legal needs disclaimers. Customer Support needs documentation and product changes towards self-serve. Solution engineers need templates and automation. DevOps demands that the team uses new deployment processes. Engineering says that test coverage is terrible.

The reality is that most of these requests will be "justified". In design thinking terms, all these things are desirable for a specific audience. And another reality is that all teams are constrained. A consistent way to make sure that cross-functional partners have clear guidance is to put everything into perspective by cross-referencing it with the product strategy.

If the strategy says that the core user persona should receive manual assistance, self-serve features won't be particularly important. For products in early, experimental stages, test coverage may not be the most important thing. If working in a highly regulated industry, compliance requests are probably a critical component.

Being consistent in adhering to the core strategy helps both the product team as well as their cross-functional counterparts to know what to expect and how to work together.

## Say No to Good Ideas
The main goal of the product team is to build a cohesive product. Cohesion in this context does not require every *good* idea to be implemented - it means that the *right* ideas should be implemented.

![image name](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/docs-assets/pom/saying-no-to-good-ideas.PNG)

Most product teams quickly understand how to spot "bad" ideas and refrain from working on them. Fewer teams develop strong skills on how to pick a subset from a large set of "good" ideas in a way that the picked ideas create a meaningful product. What makes this so hard is that there is no "right" choice - combinations of different choices lead to different products and have their related consequences.

The above is another reason why it is critically important to have a strong, clear product strategy that is rooted in the understanding of which user problems are at the heart of the team's efforts. Often going back to the main problems helps to make these tough decisions.

> "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are. You have to pick carefully. I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done. Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things."
> 
> Steve Jobs

# **Product Strategy** 
Product strategy is crucial for modern product organizations, serving as a guiding framework for development and management that aligns with broader business objectives. It ensures product teams focus on long-term goals rather than merely reacting to short-term demands, enhancing business value and providing direction for resource allocation.

At the highest level, an organization's mission defines its core values and purpose, while vision statements outline long-term aspirations. Product strategy acts as the bridge between these high-level concepts and the tactical aspects of product development, typically focusing on a mid-term horizon of one to two years.

Understanding the product life cycle--comprising phases such as Idea and Concept, Development, Launch, Growth, Maturity, and Decline--is essential for crafting an effective strategy. Each phase presents unique challenges and opportunities, necessitating an adaptable strategy that evolves with changing business conditions and user needs.

Creating a product strategy begins by translating overarching business goals into actionable objectives. This involves engaging with stakeholders, reviewing documentation, and employing both top-down and bottom-up approaches. Key elements to address include defining the target audience, understanding user needs, and aligning with business goals. For instance, a strategy that targets power users will differ significantly from one aimed at beginners.

Various frameworks, such as the Product Vision Board and GIST (Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks), can assist teams in articulating their product strategy. Once established, the strategy should be transformed into an actionable roadmap, categorizing development tasks into "Now," "Next," and "Later" buckets for prioritization.

Regular review and adjustment of the strategy are vital, as it is created with incomplete information and must remain responsive to new insights. A well-crafted product strategy provides a framework for decision-making, resource allocation, and prioritization, enabling teams to create user-centric products that drive business growth.

**Benefits of a Well-Executed Product Strategy**
1. **Enhancing Business Value**: Aligning product development with broader business strategies ensures that each effort contributes to the organization's financial objectives and market position.

2. **Effective Resource Allocation**: A strong product strategy guides resource allocation, helping teams make informed decisions about investments in new features, technology upgrades, and operational scaling.

3. **Clear Planning and Communication**: A well-defined strategy outlines the steps needed to achieve goals, serving as a communication tool across teams to ensure alignment on direction and priorities.

4. **Creating a Sense of Purpose**: A robust product strategy fosters a sense of belonging among teams by clarifying how their efforts contribute to overarching goals and the necessary collaboration for progress.

In conclusion, product strategy is essential for guiding product development in alignment with business objectives, meeting user needs, and adapting to evolving conditions. It equips teams with the clarity and purpose needed to drive successful, user-centric products.

# Mission vs. Vision vs. Strategy
It is important to understand a bit of terminology before going deep into the topic of strategy.

## General Definition
**Mission:** A mission generally exists at the organisation level, not at product level. Missions outline the core values and purpose, the **main reason why the organisation exists**. Notable examples are Disney's "entertain, inform and inspire people around the globe" or Google's "organise the world's information".

**Vision:** Vision statements are set on either organisation or product level. At organisational level, they are often used interchangeably with mission statements. At the product level, a product *vision* provides a **long-term, aspirational goal of one or multiple product(s)**. The product vision tends to be *relatively* stable and serves as a guiding star for all product-related decisions over multiple years.

> Example: UPTIMIZE Vision

![image name](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/docs-assets/pom/uptimize-vision.PNG)

**Strategy**: A *strategy* can also exist at organisation and product level. The strategy is a high-level plan that outlines what a *group of people* aims to achieve over a mid-term period (often a year or two). It aligns user needs with business objectives and commercial realities, serving as **a connection between the vision and the tactical day-to-day activities** ( which in the case of developing products would be things such as product discovery and product delivery.

## Product Vision vs. Product Strategy
When talking about vision and strategy at the product level, it is important to understand that the terms are connected, but that there are differences between a product vision and a product strategy.

**Scope and Detail:** The product vision should be broad and aspirational, providing a long-term purpose that everyone in the organisation can rally around. In contrast, the product strategy is more detailed, outlining specific actions and initiatives needed to realise the vision.

**Time Horizon**: A product vision is long-term and does not usually change unless there is a significant shift in the company's overall direction. Product strategy is generally aimed at 12-24 months and can be more fluid, adapting to changes in market conditions, competitive pressures, and internal priorities.

**Function**: While the vision serves to inspire and provide high-level direction, the strategy is about giving more actionable guidance to one or more teams. It bridges the gap between where the product is currently and where the organisation wants it to be over the period of a year or two.

**Stakeholders**: The product vision is generally communicated to all stakeholders, including customers, investors, and employees to create a shared understanding of what the organisation aims to achieve. The strategy is typically shown less broadly, used by product teams and strategic planners to guide the discovery and development process for one or more products specifically.

By clearly distinguishing between the product vision and the product strategy, and understanding the role of each, product organisations can ensure their products not only meet market needs but also drive business growth.

# Creating a product strategy 
There are as many ways to create a strategy as there are people in leadership positions. In the end, devising, formulating and executing a strategy is something that needs to work for the individuals involved and the organisation that is behind it.

## Translate overall business goals 
The first step in crafting a strong product strategy must be to understand and translate the overall goals of the organisation. This requires a thorough understanding of the organisation's overarching vision and strategic priorities. Throughout the process, these high-level goals should contribute to form a product strategy via actionable and specific objectives that align with the organisation's mission and drive its success. 

Teams working on product strategy should begin by reviewing available documentation on the organisations's goals, such as strategic plans, mission statements, and executive communications. Engaging in discussions with key stakeholders, including senior management, can help to gain clarity on the intent and priority of these goals. It is particularly important to understand the rationale behind each goal 

In cases where organisational goals are non-existent, unclear, or contradictory, seeking clarification from leadership is critically important. It is unlikely that a strong product strategy can be developed without discussions to define and articulate the company goals more precisely, so teams need to engage with various stakeholders to gather insights and build a consensus on what the goals should be. 

In cases where organisational goals are contradictory, analyse the conflicting elements and identify their underlying causes. Be aware that contradictory goals can "hide" within overly generic goals  ("we serve ALL users" is a common scenario). Engage in dialogue with decision-makers to resolve these contradictions. Develop a realistic, coherent and unified set of goals that all parts of the organisation can align with. 

Ultimately, the process of setting objectives in alignment with organisational goals requires continuous communication, regular review, and a flexible approach to adapt to evolving business landscapes. Maintaining an open dialogue with leadership and fostering a collaborative environment across departments ensures that objectives remain aligned with the company's strategic direction. 

## Create / adapt the strategy
Regardless of the individual circumstances, there are a few foundation principles that should be considered when creating a product strategy. 

### Acknowledge the level of certainty
Every strategy is created with incomplete information and is a bet on the future, product strategy is not different. Like a game plan in football or a battle plan in the military, a product strategy suffers from a non-trivial amount of uncertainty (and rarely survives the first contact). When developing the product strategy, it is important to be explicit about which assumptions and clear evidence went into the strategy. 

### Build on validated problems
An often-made mistake is to create a product strategy before a set of validated problems have been confirmed that are worth tackling. Doing so doesn't require a full Product Discovery effort, but "I think this is great" does not constitute sufficient evidence that a problem is worth solving and a product strategy is needed. 

> **Note:** Building on validated problems does not mean having users asking for something specifically. Henry Ford is one of the most famous examples: He correctly identified that horse-centered transportation was causing lots of issues (= validated problem) and he did follow a user-centric model. He understood the real pain points - and created something new (the internal combustion engine) that relieved the user's pain points better than any known solutions. 

### Combining top-down and bottom-up
Two fundamental methods for strategy creation are the top-down and bottom-up approaches, each having its strengths and weaknesses. While more cumbersome in the beginning, a combination of these two approaches generally leads to a more holistic and robust product strategy. 

In a top-down approach, the strategy is primarily driven by the organisation's overarching goals and senior management's vision. While top-down strategic planning can be effective in maintaining a clear and unified organisational direction, it also risks being out of touch with operational realities and less responsive to market changes.  

Bottom-up strategic planning is a more inclusive approach where ideas and initiatives originate from the lower levels of an organisation, such as frontline employees who are closer to the customers and day-to-day operations. These ideas are then aggregated and refined as they move up the hierarchy for broader strategic alignment. 

By combining the top-down and bottom-up approaches, organisations can create a product strategy that is both visionary and grounded in practical, actionable insights. While requiring more communication and overall effort, an integrative approach can enhance adaptability, foster innovation, and drive sustained competitive advantage. Implementing a combined approach to strategic planning provides a holistic view of organisational capabilities and market conditions, enhancing strategic effectiveness and responsiveness. However, it demands careful management to ensure that the benefits of integration do not become overshadowed by the complexities of execution. 

## Must-have topics to address in a product strategy 
Different teams put different things into their strategies - this depends on the framework in use. Here are the most common ones that are valid across methods. 

### Target Group
Understanding the target group is fundamental to creating a product that resonates with users. The target group determines what behaviour and preferences need to be considered as well as what communication channels are most effective. Not defining a specific (and somewhat narrow) target group of the product will likely result in a product that misses a clear value proposition. 

**Example:** Gmail started out as a clear consumer offering. It was clear to the team that the B2B target group would require many additional features and that Gmail as a product was not a strong fit for this target group. Only years later a new product was introduced that eventually introduced email as an offer from Google to their B2B customers. 

### User Needs
Addressing user needs (from within the chosen target group!) is vital for creating a product that provides value. The strategy must outline which problems are being solved or which needs are fulfilled that create value to users. 

### User Interface and User Experience
The physical appearance of a digital product, encompassing UI and UX, plays a significant role in user engagement and satisfaction. Many day-to-day choices will depend on fundamental UX strategy such as form factor, pricing model and design language. 

**Example:** With ubiquitous connectivity, one of the key decisions for each product is how to address the mobile use case. Depending on the strategic decision there, many other decisions will then follow. 

### Business Goals
As outlined before, any product strategy can only work if it aligns with the broader business goals. It likely needs some form of commercial evaluation and references on how it will benefit overall business priorities. 

Addressing at least target group, user needs, physical appearance, and business goals in a product strategy makes it a lot more likely that the product strategy becomes comprehensive and focused.

# Popular frameworks
There are many popular frameworks in use when it comes to Product Strategy. Some of them have been around for a while, some are also used for non-digital products or company strategy, some are on a specific organisation type or size. Here is a list of the most applicable frameworks to Merck, note that these can be combined in a variety of ways.

### Product Vision Board (Roman Pichler)
Roman Pichler's Product Vision Board is a strategic tool used to define and communicate the high-level vision for a product. Although it is called a "Vision Board" it is primarily geared at a more comprehensive Product Strategy. Here's a concise summary of its key components:

1. Vision: The overarching goal or purpose of the product.
2. Target Group: The primary users or customers the product is intended for.
3. Needs: The main problem or need the product addresses for the target group.
4. Product: A brief description of the product and its key features.
5. Business Goals: The objectives the company aims to achieve with the product.

This visual framework helps product teams align on the core elements of their product strategy and provides a foundation for more detailed planning. It's designed to be simple and adaptable, allowing teams to easily update and refine their product vision as they gather more information and insights.

### DIBB (Spotify)
The DIBB framework is a decision-making and prioritization model. DIBB stands for Data, Insight, Belief, and Bet. Here's a concise summary of its components:

1. Data: Objective, quantitative information collected about users, market trends, or product performance.
2. Insight: Interpretations and conclusions drawn from the data, often revealing patterns or opportunities.
3. Belief: A hypothesis or theory about what action should be taken based on the insights.
4. Bet: The actual decision or investment made to test the belief.

This framework encourages data-driven decision-making while also allowing for intuition and calculated risk-taking. It helps product teams balance analytical thinking with creative problem-solving, and provides a structured approach to turning raw data into actionable strategies.

### GIST / MuST (Itamar Gilad)
The MUST/GIST framework, developed by Itamar Gilad, combines idea prioritization with iterative development. Here's a summary of its key components:

MUST (Metrics, Urgency, Strengths, Tests):
1. Metrics: The impact the idea is expected to have on key performance indicators.
2. Urgency: How time-sensitive the idea is.
3. Strengths: The unique advantages or capabilities the team has to execute the idea.
4. Tests: Past experiments or data that support the idea's potential.

GIST (Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks):
1. Goals: High-level, measurable objectives for the product.
2. Ideas: Potential solutions or features to achieve the goals.
3. Steps: Small, testable iterations to validate ideas.
4. Tasks: Specific, actionable work items to implement each step.

This framework aims to balance long-term vision with short-term, data-driven execution. It emphasizes continuous learning and adaptation, helping teams prioritize ideas effectively and break them down into manageable, testable increments.

# **Product Delivery** 

## **Purpose**

Agile Product Delivery is a customer-centric approach to defining, building, and releasing a continuous flow of valuable products and services to customers. It is one of the seven core competencies of the Lean Enterprise, which are essential for achieving Business Agility. The goal of Agile Product Delivery is to rapidly increase an enterprise's ability to deliver innovative products and services that meet the needs of their customers.

By adopting a customer-centric mindset, enterprises can make decisions that consider the effect on end users. This involves understanding customer needs, segmenting the market, and thinking like the customer. Design Thinking is a key component of Agile Product Delivery, which involves understanding the problem, defining the solution, and delivering it to the market. It follows a process of discovery, definition, development, and delivery.

Prioritizing the ART (Agile Release Train) Backlog is an important aspect of Agile Product Delivery. The ART Backlog is the holding area for upcoming features that will address user needs and deliver business benefits. Features are prioritized based on their economic value and the time it takes to implement them.

Agile Product Delivery also emphasizes the importance of developing on cadence and releasing on demand. This means that development teams work in fixed timeboxes called Program Increments (PIs) and release their products or features whenever they are ready, rather than waiting for a specific release date. This allows for more flexibility and faster delivery of value to customers.

Building a Continuous Delivery Pipeline with DevOps is another key aspect of Agile Product Delivery. This involves automating the process of building, testing, and deploying software, allowing for faster and more reliable delivery of products and services. Continuous Integration, Continuous Deployment, and Release on Demand are practices commonly used in Agile Product Delivery.

Overall, Agile Product Delivery is crucial for achieving Business Agility. By adopting a customer-centric approach, prioritizing the backlog based on economic value, and implementing practices like continuous delivery, enterprises can deliver innovative products and services that meet the needs of their customers.

##**What is an Epic?**
An Epic is a container for a significant development initiative.

There are two types of epics:
ART Epic: an initiative that is constrained to a single ART.
Portfolio Epic: involves more than one ART.

Due to their considerable scope and impact, epics require the definition of a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) and approval by Portfolio Management (PM).
The goal of layer is to optimize the portfolio of all investment opportunities (maximize benefits for given investment)

**A minimum viable product (MVP) is an early version of a new product, service, or Solution that requires the minimum investment necessary to validate the Epic hypothesis.**

Epics typically would take more than one PI (3 month to 12 months) to be implemented.

# Day 1

- **Business context**- A Business Owner or senior executive provides an overview of the current state of the business, shares the Portfolio Vision, and discusses how existing solutions address customer needs.
- **Product vision**- Product Management presents the current vision, highlighting upcoming features and any relevant milestones.
- **Architecture vision and development practices**- The System Architect presents the architecture vision, and a senior development manager may introduce Agile-supportive changes to development practices.
- **Planning context**- The Release Train Engineer (RTE) presents the planning process and expected outcomes.
- **Team breakouts**- Teams estimate their capacity for each Iteration, identify backlog items needed for features, and create draft plans visible to all. They also identify risks and dependencies and draft their initial team PI objectives 

# Day 2

- **Team breakouts & Speed dating with the other Teams**- Teams continue refining their plans, addressing risks and dependencies, and finalizing their team PI objectives. This include sessions with Management as part of feedback and Program risks.
- **Management review**- Management reviews Teams plans, risks and objectives to management for alignment and discuss any potential changes in scope or resourcing.

# Day 3

- **Team breakouts**- Problem-solving workshop (if necessary): Teams receives feedback from management and address any challenges or issues that may impact their ability to achieve their objectives.
- **ROAMing Risks**- The identified risks and impediments are discussed and categorized using the ROAM (Resolved, Owned, Accepted, Mitigated) approach. Each risk is evaluated and placed into one of these categories based on its status and potential impact.
- **Confidence Vote**- Once the ART (Agile Release Train) PI Risks have been addressed, teams vote on their confidence in meeting their team PI objectives. This vote helps assess the team's level of confidence in their plan and their ability to deliver. This is provided by using numbers from 1 to 5 (no confidence, little, good, high, very high) 

## **Why do we use PI Objectives?**

- **Immediate feedback**- Team PI Objectives provide confirmation to business leaders that teams understand desired outcomes.
- **Decentralized decisions**- The business value of the team PI Objectives promote decentralized decision-making should the team need to adjust planned work.
- **Predictability without specificity**- Committing to PI objectives instead of a specific Feature or Story enhances agility. Commitment to PI objectives allows the team and the Product Owner to modify the planned work based on discovery and Customer input and still achieve the business value.
- **Commitment**- Teams, not business leaders, commit to the PI objectives.


In order to measure ART predictability, we need to assign business value to PI Objectives from 1 to 10. We add this values to committed and uncommitted ones.
These are the considerations used for this activity.

- **Regulatory Value**- Legal or infrastructure functionality that, if not deployed, can result in fines, revenue loss, or damage to the Enterprise brand
- **Commercial value**- Product/service functionality that brings new revenue or maintains existing revenue
- **Market value**- Functionality that differentiates the product/service from competing products/services and new functionality needed to stay competitive
- **Efficiency value**- Functionality that reduces operating costs, including technical debt or improvements in the pipeline
- **Future value**- Functionality that focuses on enabling or realizing future value, including Enablers, proof of concepts (POCs), and research spikes

The **Team Backlog** contains User and Enabler Stories that originate from the ART Backlog, as well as Stories that arise locally from the team's local context. 
It may include other work items as well, representing all the things a team needs to do to advance their portion of the system.
The PO is responsible for the Team Backlog. 
Since it includes both User and Enabler Stories, it's essential to allocate capacity in a way that balances investments across conflicting needs.
The allocation accounts for both the needs of the ART and the team.
Because User Stories focus on the user as the subject of interest and not the system, the recommendation is to form them in user voice format, as follows:

As a (user role), I want (activity), so that (business value).

By using the user voice format, the teams are guided to understand:
- Who is using the system
- What they are doing it

The Three Cs for writing good Stories include 'card,' 'conversation,' and 'confirmation".

INVEST Model also helps on writing good quality stories:
I - Independent (among other Stories)
N - Negotiable (a flexible statement of intent, not a contract)
V - Valuable (providing a valuable vertical slice to the Customer)
E - Estimable (small and negotiable)
S - Small (fits within an iteration)
T - Testable (understood enough to know how to test it)

Relating Features and stories to Personas improves design.

Another good approach is to follow these 10 patterns to split stories and features that are too big to fit in an iteration or PI:

- **Workflow steps** - Identify specific steps that a user takes to accomplish a specific workflow and then implement the workflow in incremental stages.
- **Business rule variations** - At first glance, some Stories seem fairly simple. However, sometimes the business rules are more complex or extensive than the first glance revealed.
In this case, it might be useful to break the Story into several Stories to handle the business rule complexity.
- **Major Effort** - Sometimes a Story can be split into several parts where most of the effort will go towards implementing the first one.
- **Simple/complex** - When the team is discussing a Story and the Story seems to be getting larger and larger ("What about x?" "Have you considered y?"), stop and ask,
"What's the simplest solution that can possibly fulfill the need?". Capture that simple version as its own Story, and then break out all the variations and complexities into their own Stories.
- **Variations in data** Data variations and data sources are another source of scope and complexity. Consider adding Stories just in time after building the simplest version.
- **Data entry methods** - Sometimes complexity is in the user interface rather than the functionality itself. 

In that case, split the Story to build it with the simplest possible UI and then build the richer UI later.

- **Defer system qualities** - Sometimes, the initial implementation isn't all that hard, and the major part of the effort is in making it fast, or reliable, or more precise, or more scalable.
However, the team can learn a lot from the base implementation, and it should have some value to a user who wouldn't otherwise be able to do it all. In this case, break the Story into successive qualities.
- **Operations** - Words like 'manage' or 'control' are a giveaway that the Story covers multiple operations, which can offer a natural way to split the Story. For example, Create Read Update Delete (CRUD).
- **Use case scenarios** - If use cases have been developed to represent complex user-to-system or system-to-system interaction, then the Story can often be split according to the individual scenarios of the use case.
- **Break out a spike** - In some cases,a Story may be too large or overly complex, or perhaps the implementation is poorly understood. In that case, build a technical or functional spike to figure it out, then split the Stories based on that result.

## **Team Events associated to team workflow:**
# **Iteration Planning**

This event occurs at the beginning of each iteration and involves the entire team. 
The purpose is to plan and prioritize the work to be done in the upcoming iteration.
The team reviews the backlog, selects the items to be worked on, estimates effort, and creates a plan for the iteration.

Agenda:

- Establishing capacity
- Story analysis and estimating
- Detailing Stories
- Developing Iteration Goals
- Committing to Iteration Goals


Establishing capacity before historical data exists:

- Give the team eight points for every full-time developer and tester on the team; adjust for part-time individuals. 
- Subtract one point for every team member vacation day and holiday.
- Find a small Story that would take about a half day to develop and a half day to test and validate, and assign it one point.
- Estimate every other Story relative to that one-point Story.

Example: A seven-person team composed of three developers, two testers, one PO, and one SM/TC, with no vacation, and a two-week Iteration Note: Exclude PO and SM/TC from the calculation.
Estimated capacity = 5 x 8 points = 40 points/Iteration

A Story point is a number that represents:

- Volume - How much there is
- Complexity - How difficult it is
- Knowledge - What we know
- Uncertainty - What's not known

Each story's size (effort) is estimated relative to the smallest story, which is assigned a size of 'one'.
A modified Fibonacci sequence (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20, 40, 100) is applied that reflects the inherent uncertainty in estimating, especially large numbers (for example, 20, 40, 100).
One preferred technique for estimating is poker planning which is easier and faster.


Another good practice for teams to focus is to write Iteration Goals which provide clarity, commitment, and management information.
**A team meets its commitments**:
By doing everything they said they would do - or- 
By immediately raising the concern if it isn't feasible to do so.

Iteration Goals often reflect the following:

- Features, Feature slices, and Feature aspects, such as research and necessary infrastructure
- Business or technical Milestones
- Architectural, infrastructure, exploration, and compliance activities
- Routine jobs and other activities, such as maintenance and documentation


# **Daily Stand-up**: 

This is a short, daily meeting where team members provide updates on their progress, discuss any obstacles or challenges they are facing, and coordinate their work for the day. The purpose is to promote transparency, alignment, and collaboration within the team.

# **Backlog Refinement**:

This is an ongoing activity where the team collaborates to refine and prioritize the backlog items. 
They clarify requirements, estimate effort, and ensure that the backlog is ready for future iterations. 
The purpose is to ensure that the team has a clear understanding of the work to be done and that the backlog is well-prepared for future planning events.

# **Iteration Review**: 

At the end of each iteration, the team holds an iteration review. They showcase the work they have completed during the iteration and gather feedback from stakeholders. The purpose is to demonstrate progress, gather feedback, and ensure alignment with stakeholders.
Product Owners validate results according to attached evidence and Team Definition of Done (DoD).
Definition of Done (DoD) is a set of criteria that a work product or increment must meet in order to be considered complete.

# **Team metrics**:

Flow Metrics can be collected at team-level to review them during Iteration review.

# **Iteration Retrospective**:

This event takes place at the end of each iteration and focuses on continuous improvement. The team reflects on their performance, identifies what went well and what could be improved, and creates action items to address areas of improvement. The purpose is to foster a culture of learning and continuous improvement within the team.
POs are required to attend team events. POs are responsible for participation and leadership for:

# **Executing the Planning Increment**

From the ART perspective there three additional ceremonies that occurs at ART level.

## **Agile Team Coach Sync**

- Agile Team Coach Sync is typically held on a weekly basis, although it can be more frequent if needed.
- The meeting is timeboxed to ensure it stays within a specific duration.
- It provides an opportunity for Scrum Masters and Agile Team Coaches to collaborate, share information, and discuss any challenges or issues faced by the teams.
- The focus of Agile Team Coach Sync is on risks, dependencies, progress, and impediments of the teams and the Agile Release Train (ART).
- It helps ensure coordination and alignment between teams, identify and address any obstacles that may impact team performance, and provide support and guidance to Scrum Masters and Agile Team Coaches.
- Agile Team Coach Sync is followed by a "meet-after" session, which allows for more in-depth problem-solving and discussion of specific issues or topics.

## **Product owner Sync**

- PO Sync is a weekly meeting for Product Owners and Product Management to align and collaborate.
- It occurs once or twice per week and lasts 30 to 60 minutes.
- The purpose is to track progress towards PI objectives, discuss feature development, and prepare the backlog for PI Planning.
- It can be facilitated by a Product Manager, RTE, or both.
- Activities include refining the program backlog, preparing for system demos, and evaluating feature quality.

## **PI System Demo**

- The System Demo in SAFe is an event where stakeholders can see the new features delivered by Agile teams on the ART.
- It provides an integrated view of the solution and allows for feedback and assessment of progress.
- The demo is an opportunity to measure the value achieved and gather input from stakeholders, sponsors, and customers.
- It helps ensure transparency, collaboration, and alignment with business objectives.
- The System Demo showcases the functionality, usability, and suitability of the solution in a production-like environment.
- Stakeholders can evaluate the features, provide feedback, and guide the direction of the solution.
- The demo promotes stakeholder engagement, keeps them informed, and allows for adjustments based on feedback.
- It is an essential part of the SAFe framework to measure progress, assess value, and make informed decisions for the next iterations.


## **Innovation and Planning iteration**

The IP Iteration occurs every PI and serves multiple purposes. 
It acts as an estimating guard band for meeting PI Objectives, and provides dedicated time for innovation, continuous learning, and Inspect and Adapt and the upcoming PI Planning event.

SAFe has an intense focus on continuous value delivery for Customers, and people are busy working on the Features they committed to during PI Planning. Every Iteration counts and the teams are mostly heads-down, delivering near-term value.
One Iteration after another, the Solution marches closer to market.
The attention on Solution delivery is intense and unrelenting. Of course, a focus on delivery can lead to a lack of focus on innovation. 
Given the constant urgency for delivery, there's a risk that the 'tyranny of the urgent' will override any opportunity to innovate. To address this, SAFe provides dedicated IP Iterations.

# **Inspect and Adapt Event**

The Inspect and Adapt (I&A) is a significant event held at the end of each Program Increment (PI) in SAFe. It involves a structured problem-solving workshop where teams reflect on the current state of the solution and identify improvement backlog items.
The I&A consists of three parts: PI System Demo, quantitative and qualitative measurement, and retrospective and problem-solving workshop.

## **PI System Demo:**

At the end of each PI, teams demonstrate the current state of the solution to stakeholders. This demo is led by Product Management, Product Owners, and the System Team.
It provides an opportunity to showcase the solution's progress and receive feedback from stakeholders

## **Quantitative and Qualitative Measurement**

Teams meet with their Business Owners to self-assess the business value achieved for each objective. These assessments are rolled up to the ART Predictability Measure, which shows the planned versus actual business value. 
Teams also assess their performance against stated objectives and calculate an ART percent achievement score.

The three measurement domains are defined as follows:

- Outcomes - Do our Solutions meet the needs of our Customers and the business?
- Flow - How efficient is the organization at delivering value to the Customer?
- Competency - How proficient is the organization in the practices that enable Business Agility?

<img src=" https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/docs-assets/pom/I&A2.PNG " width="700"/>

## **Retrospective and Problem-Solving Workshop**

After a short retrospective, teams systematically address improvement areas identified during the I&A. This workshop allows teams to reflect on their performance, identify root causes of issues, and generate improvement backlog items.
The Inspect and Adapt event is a crucial part of the SAFe framework, providing a mechanism for continuous improvement and ensuring that the ART stays on track. It allows teams to adjust, measure progress, and make informed decisions based on feedback and data.
For addressing systemic problems, a structured, root cause problem-solving workshop is held by the ART. 
Root cause analysis provides a set of problem-solving tools used to identify the actual causes of a problem, rather than just addressing the symptoms. 
The session is typically facilitated by the RTE, in a timebox of two hours or less.

## **Agile Team Ways of Working (Devops mindset)**

At MDAO most Teams adopt DevOps mindset so in this section we are going to provide information about  it:

DevOps is a set of practices that combines software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops) to improve collaboration, increase the frequency and quality of deployments, and enhance the overall efficiency and effectiveness of the software development process. It aims to bridge the gap between development and operations teams, enabling faster and more reliable delivery of software solutions.
The Continuous Delivery Pipeline (CDP) is a concept in SAFe that represents the end-to-end workflows, activities, and automation needed to guide new functionality from ideation to an on-demand release of value. It encompasses the entire value stream, from the initial idea to the deployment of the solution to production. The CDP enables organizations to continuously deliver new functionality, validate it, and release it to meet customer demands.
Essentially the CDP represents all the steps required to move an idea across the Value Stream to the Customer.

Empowered Teams Operations, please refer to the [Empowered Teams](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/empowered-teams/operations/) documentation. 

DevOps and the CDP are closely related. DevOps provides the mindset, culture, and practices necessary to enable the continuous delivery of software solutions. It emphasizes collaboration, automation, and continuous improvement. The CDP, on the other hand, represents the technical implementation of DevOps principles and practices. It provides the structure and automation needed to support the continuous delivery of value, from idea to production.
**Having a good balance and prioritization between operations and engineering is crucial** because it enables the seamless integration of these two worlds, fostering collaboration, efficiency, and overall success. 

By aligning their backlogs and priorities, organizations can ensure smooth operations, timely delivery of value, and a focus on continuous improvement. This balance allows for effective resource allocation, reduced bottlenecks, improved communication, and ultimately, the ability to deliver high-quality products and services that meet customer needs.

**Here is a summary of the components:**

**Continuous Exploration (CE)**

This component focuses on creating alignment on what needs to be built. It involves understanding market problems or customer needs, analyzing ideas, converging on requirements for a Minimum Viable Product (MVP), and prioritizing them in the ART Backlog.

**Continuous Integration (CI)**

CI focuses on taking features from the ART backlog and implementing them. It involves refining features, implementing them through Agile Teams, committing completed work to version control, building and integrating the work, and testing it end-to-end before validation in a staging environment.

**Continuous Deployment (CD)**

CD takes the changes from the staging environment and deploys them to production. It involves verifying and monitoring the changes in production, making features available to customers, and allowing the business to release them at the appropriate time.


**Quality and security must be built in at every step. Solutions must be architected for rapid change. Governance and compliance controls must be streamlined and automated.**

The right automation must be in place to enable lightning-fast flow and on-demand processing. 
Continuous Security emphasizes practices such as security by design, threat modeling, security-as-code, and automation in vulnerability scanning, penetration testing, and intrusion detection. 
The related practices in this domain are expressed 'as code'--infrastructure-as-code, security-as-code, and compliance-as-code, for instance. 
The Configuration Management practice domain represents an 'everything as code' approach to defining solutions, their runtime environments, and the systems that pull them through the continuous delivery pipeline.

**Release on Demand (RoD)**

RoD is the ability to make value available to customers based on market and business needs. It allows for the release of value together or in a staggered fashion, controlling the risk associated with each release. 
RoD also **includes activities that preserve solution stability and enduring value after release.**

After deployment, maintenance and support can be addressed through the practice of **"Stabilize and Operate."**
This practice focuses on ensuring the stability, reliability, and ongoing operation of the deployed solution. 

Here are some key aspects of the Stabilize and Operate practice:

- **Monitoring and Incident Management**

Implementing robust monitoring systems to detect and respond to incidents promptly. This includes setting up alerts, monitoring logs, and establishing incident management processes to investigate and resolve issues.

- **Problem Management** 

Identifying and addressing the root causes of recurring incidents or problems. This involves conducting thorough investigations, implementing corrective actions, and continuously improving the solution to prevent similar issues from occurring in the future.

- **Change Management**

Managing changes to the deployed solution in a controlled and systematic manner. This includes evaluating and approving change requests, conducting impact assessments, and ensuring proper testing and validation before implementing changes.

- **Release Management**

Planning and coordinating the release of updates, patches, and new features to the production environment. This involves managing release schedules, coordinating with development teams, and ensuring smooth deployments with minimal disruption to users. 
Automating the release management process can help streamline the deployment of software to different environments (i.e. via Azure Devops Pipelines)

   - **Capacity Management**

Monitoring and managing the resources and infrastructure required to support the solution. 
This includes capacity planning, performance monitoring, and scaling resources as needed to meet demand and maintain optimal performance.

   - **Security and Compliance**

Implementing security measures and ensuring compliance with relevant regulations and standards. 
This includes conducting regular security assessments, implementing security controls, and staying up to date with security best practices.

**By following the Stabilize and Operate practice, organizations can effectively maintain and operate their deployed solutions, ensuring their stability, reliability, and ongoing value to the customers.**

As part of this we can implement Service Level Agreements (SLAs), so that you can ensure that the solution operates within defined parameters and meets the required service levels. 
SLAs also provide a framework for measuring and monitoring the performance of the solution, allowing you to identify and address any deviations or issues promptly.

# **Product Success** 
Product success extends far beyond the initial launch of a digital product. While launches are significant milestones, true success encompasses user adoption, satisfaction, financial viability, and long-term sustainability. This journey from launch to success requires continuous iteration, user engagement, and effective problem-solving.

Measuring product performance is essential for informed decision-making and ongoing improvement. Frameworks like AARRR (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue) and HEART (Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, Task success) provide valuable insights into user behavior and product health. A combination of quantitative and qualitative data collection, with a focus on scalable analytics, enables teams to assess product performance accurately.

Achieving product-market fit is crucial, as it indicates that a product meets strong demand. This often necessitates iteration and experimentation, along with a willingness to pivot based on user feedback. A well-defined target audience guides product decisions and feature prioritization, ensuring alignment with user needs.

When corporate goals conflict with user interests, product teams face challenges that can jeopardize success. Navigating these conflicts requires reassessing assumptions, quantifying misalignments, proposing alternative strategies, and focusing on harm mitigation when necessary.

Engagement with users is a vital aspect of product success. Various engagement models, ranging from self-serve to high-touch solutions, help improve adoption and satisfaction. The choice of model should consider product complexity, user characteristics, and available resources.

As products evolve, they can accumulate complexity in both architecture and operations, potentially diverting time away from innovation. To manage this complexity, teams should conduct time allocation analyses, catalog recurring issues, and implement best practices to prevent further accumulation.

Achieving product success is a multifaceted, ongoing process that demands persistence, constant measurement, targeted user engagement, and effective complexity management. By focusing on these areas, product teams can navigate the challenges of digital product development, ensuring their solutions resonate with users and drive sustainable business growth.

In summary, launching a product is only the beginning. Success is earned through a commitment to continuous improvement, user satisfaction, and strategic alignment with business goals, ultimately transforming initial launches into lasting achievements.

# Understanding Strategy
Having and knowning a clear, well-defined strategy is the first necessary step for the success of any digital product. Without it, even the most talented teams can find themselves expending effort in the wrong direction, leading to wasted resources and missed opportunities. This is why strategic goals should be part of everyones myImpact commitments.

## Business Strategy vs Product Strategy
It is important to distinguish between different levels of strategy.

The first level is the **business strategy**. This is often set at very high levels of the organisation and driven by commercial realities. It may include creating new revenue channels, expanding market share, reducing costs or improving internal processes.

The second level is the **product strategy**. This is derived from the business strategy as well as from insights coming out of the Product Discovery activities. This is outlined in detail in [Product Strategy](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-strategy/). Product strategy can have multiple levels. At Merck, there is a high-level product vision for UPTIMIZE as a whole as well as product strategies for the individual components that make up the UPTIMIZE ecosystem.

## Understanding and Analysing the Overall Strategy
In many situations, product teams are tasked with executing specific parts of higher-level strategies that have already been defined by leadership. While this provides a clear direction, it's important for teams to understand, analyse, and - when necessary - constructively challenge the established strategies. This process ensures that the strategies are feasible, aligned with business and user realities, and that the team is capable of delivering the desired business value.

Product teams (especially newly formed product teams) must thoroughly review the available documentation on higher-level strategy. This includes strategic plans, executive communications, and any presentations or memos that outline the strategy. However, documents alone rarely tell the full story. Teams should engage in discussions with key stakeholders, including senior management and cross-functional partners, to gain deeper insights into the rationale behind strategic decisions and the expected outcomes.

Understanding the strategy also involves mapping it against the current product and internal capabilities. Teams should analyse how the strategy corelates with user needs, competitive pressures, and technological trends. This analysis helps identify potential gaps or misalignments between existing strategies and real-world conditions.

Throughout this process, teams may uncover inconsistencies or potential issues. These could range from conflicting objectives to overly optimistic assumptions about use or internal capabilities. To effectively identify these issues, teams should:
- Take the time to have dedicated product strategy sessions and discuss their understanding of the higher-level strategy.
- Clarify any questions with leadership or key stakeholders.
- Break down the higher-level strategy into its component parts and examine each in detail.
- Cross-reference several aspects of the strategy to ensure they align and support each other.
- Seek input from team members with diverse perspectives and expertise.

## Addressing Strategic Inconsistencies
When inconsistencies or potential issues are identified, it's important to approach them constructively. It is possible that the major problem lies in how the strategy has been communicated rather than the strategy itself, so gathering data and evidence to understand the issues should come first.

If a team believes that a higher-level strategy is flawer or that it cannot be executed successfully, it's their responsibility to bring this thoughtfully and professionally to leadership's attention. Here are some approaches for effectively communicating concerns:
- Frame the discussion around shared goals and the organisation's best interests.
- Present clear, data-driven evidence that supports the concerns.
- Offer specific, actionable suggestions for addressing the issues.
- Be prepared to engage in a dialogue and answer questions about the concerns raised.
- Approach the conversation with a collaborative mindset, focusing on finding solutions.

There may be constraints or considerations that weren't immediately apparent or not fully communicated. By engaging in open and honest dialogue with leadership, teams can gain a more comprehensive understanding of the strategy and potentially contribute to its refinement.

Leadership may decide to maintain the current strategy despite the concerns raised. In these situations, teams should focus on executing the strategy to the best of their abilities while continuing to monitor its effectiveness and gather data that could inform future strategic decisions. This is often described as a *disagree and commit* principle.

The ultimate goal is to ensure that both business and product strategy are robust and effective. Product teams can play an important role in influencing the overall strategy and driving the organisation's success.

## What to do in Cases of "No Strategy"
It is possible that teams operate in a scenario where no established strategy exists. For example, this is common in greenfield initiatives or when working with very innovative technology. Areas within MDAO where this is the case are GenAI or Cloud Warehousing.

Product teams working in these areas have the opportunity and obligation to help define both business and product strategy in these cases (see chapters on Product Discovery and Product Strategy). This requires the team to build business skills, so that they can bring together technology and business thinking.

## Using Strategy to be Successful
The role of understanding strategy to achieve product success is important and clear: A well-understood strategy gives the team the necessary guardrails to tackle the most important part of product management: Taking decisions on which topics are high priority, low priority or out of scope.

# Measuring Product Performance
Measuring product performance is a key activity to create successful products.

## Working with Hypothesis
A common way to develop a product and make it successful is by using hypothesis testing.

A team generally starts by identifying the problem or opportunity you want to address, ideally aligned with the overall strategy. Then they form a **clear, testable hypothesis** that outlines how they think a positive outcome can be achieved.

> Example: A team is tasked with decreasing the time it takes for users to conduct a specific analysis. Their first *hypothesis* is that the majority of time is spent finding data - doing the analysis itself is quick. Their second *hypothesis* is that a search engine for data products will dramatically lower the time to find the relevant data - and thus decrease the overall time to conduct the analysis.

Once a team has formed their hypothesis, they gather data to prove or disprove it. This can be done through user research, surveys, or interviews to validate a hypothesis before starting development. During and after development, proving a hypothesis will most commonly done via product metrics collected directly from the product.

## Product Metric Frameworks
While the specific metrics used may vary depending on the nature of the product and its goals, several frameworks have emerged as valuable tools for assessing product performance. These frameworks provide a comprehensive view of user behaviour, engagement, and overall product health.

One widely adopted framework is AARRR, also known as "Pirate Metrics" due to its acronym's pronunciation. Developed by Dave McClure, this model focuses on five key areas: Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, and Revenue. Although it sounds commercial, it applies to most products, including internal products.
- **(A)cquisition** metrics track how users discover and start using the product, often including measures like website traffic, app downloads, or sign-up rates.
- **(A)ctivation** looks at the user's first experience and whether they find value in the product, which might be measured by completed onboarding steps or initial key actions.
- **(R)etention** focuses on ongoing engagement, tracking how often users return and continue to use the product over time.
- **(R)eferral** metrics assess how well existing users bring in new users, often through word-of-mouth or formal referral programs. 
- **(R)evenue** metrics track the financial performance of the product, including measures like conversion rates, average order value, or lifetime customer value. In cases where a product is not directly paid for, this can be extended to include other types of monetary impact such as cost per action or ROI.

Another powerful framework is HEART, developed by Google. It has a slightly different angle, focusing more on the individual user versus the sum of all users. This model examines user experience through five dimensions: Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, and Task success.
- **(H)appiness** measures user satisfaction and attitude towards the product, often through surveys or net promoter scores.
- **(E)ngagement** looks at the level of user involvement with the product, which might include metrics like session length or frequency of use.
- **(A)doption** tracks how many new users start using the product or its features.
- **(R)etention**, like the AARRR framework, focuses on continued usage over time.
- **(T)ask success** measures the efficiency, effectiveness, and error rate of user interactions with the product.


It's important to note that these frameworks do not have to be applied rigidly but can be adapted to suit the specific needs and context of each product. The key is to select metrics that offer actionable insights and align with the overall product strategy and business goals. For instance, a B2B SaaS product might focus more heavily on customer retention and annual recurring revenue, while an internal analytics product may focus on task success and word of mouth referrals.

## Collecting Data for Product Metrics
Once a decision on which metrics to use have been made, the next task is to gather the actual information.

Teams that want to understand product performance should ideally use a comprehensive approach to data collection that combines both quantitative and qualitative methods. While qualitative insights provide depth and context, quantitative data offers scalable, measurable insights that often drive decision-making.

**Qualitative data collection** is generally not the primary method to collect information on product success but still is an important complement to quantitative methods. Teams can gather qualitative insights through user interviews, focus groups, and usability testing sessions. These methods provide rich, contextual information about user experiences, preferences, and pain points. Surveys with open-ended questions and analysis of customer support interactions can also yield valuable qualitative data. While these methods don't scale as easily as quantitative approaches, they offer nuanced insights that numbers alone can't capture.

**Quantitative data collection** forms the backbone of scalable product analytics. This approach involves gathering quantitative data that can be analysed statistically to find trends, patterns, and correlations. Quantitative data is particularly valuable for understanding user behaviour at scale, tracking key performance indicators (KPIs) and making data-driven decisions.

One of the most common methods for collecting quantitative data is through direct product instrumentation. This involves adding code to the product that tracks specific user actions and events. Every button click, page view, feature usage, and user flow can be recorded and analysed. This granular data provides a detailed picture of how users interact with a product. Other good examples of quantitative data points are user acquisition and retention metrics. This requires tracking new user sign-ups, active users (daily, weekly, and monthly), and churn rates. These metrics help teams understand the product's growth trajectory and user engagement levels.

To collect and analyse this data at scale, teams rely on various technical tools. Common tools are Amplitude, Pendo or Mixpanel but a lot of vendors offer their own integrated toolsets (e.g. Foundry or Tableau). When implementing such tools, the team should define key events and metrics to track, ensure proper data governance and privacy compliance, and regularly audit data quality. This often benefits from specific expertise, which - in large product teams - can be provided by specialised data analysts.

## Using Product Data to make Decisions
The first thing that teams need to do to is to create **a clear understanding of which metrics used and what they mean**. It is not uncommon for teams to use different definitions of extremely common metrics such as MAU (monthly active users) or churn rate - this can lead to misunderstandings and (very) bad decisions. It is also not uncommon that it takes months for non-team members to fully understand what data a team collects, what it means and how it is being used.

The second important thing to do is to **process data to be useful**. Raw data is generally not directly helpful - it needs to be processed, analysed and visualised. This is somewhat intuitive to teams in MDAO given the domain they work on is in fact data products - but it is critical that the teams themselves use the same methodologies that they try to foster with their customers and users.

The third aspect is creating **strong habits of bringing data into all product conversations**. Questions like "how many users are affected by this", "which metric are we targeting with this change" or "what is the impact is" should be asked often and routinely. Team members should be expected to bring data as evidence for ideas, solutions or any other change that is being discussed. If no data is present during the discussion, it should be explicitly stated on which information decisions were made.

> Quote: *"If we have data, let's look at data. If all we have are opinions, let's go with mine."*
― Jim Barksdale (CEO, Netscape)

By combining the three aspects, product teams can use data to continuously improve their product's performance. This data-driven approach enables teams to make informed decisions, prioritise effectively, and continuously improve the product based on real user behaviour and preferences.

Regardless of the specific framework or tools chosen, the process of measuring success should be ongoing and iterative. Teams should regularly review and adjust metrics to ensure they continue to offer meaningful insights as the product evolves and conditions change.

> Example: During the scale & growth phase it makes sense to track new signups and acquisition metrics, but once a product hits maturity stage it becomes increasingly more important to focus on engagament and retention metrics. See more information about [lifecycle stages here](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/executive-summary/lifecycle/)

By maintaining a data-driven approach to product development, teams can make more informed decisions, allocate resources more effectively, and ultimately create products that truly resonate with users and drive business success.

# Product-Market-Fit 
The concept of **product-market fit (PMF)** describes a situation when a product satisfies a strong market / customer / user demand. Ideally this means that demand for the product does not have to be artificially stimulated but a natural "pull" is happening from the user base. Achieving product-market fit is often an important milestone for the success and growth of any product - external or internal. 

To achieve product-market fit, teams strongly benefit from [Product Discovery](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-discovery/). Running product discovery activities helps the team to deeply understand their target audience's needs and create a product that addresses these needs better than any alternative. When product-market fit is achieved, it typically manifests in high user engagement, strong word-of-mouth growth, and rapid user acquisition. 

> Example: UPTIMIZE Foundry achieved PMF once employees started to sign up for it based on word-of-mouth from colleagues.

Unfortunately, the path to product-market fit is rarely straightforward. It often requires iteration, experimentation, and a willingness to pivot based on user feedback and product usage.

## Target Audience 
The first step to achieving product-market-fit is having a well-defined target audience - it provides a clear focus for ongoing iterations and experiments. A well-defined target audience acts as a north star, guiding product decisions, feature prioritisation, and training and marketing efforts. It's the foundation upon which all successful products are built. 

Choosing the *initial* target audience is often done during [Product Discovery](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-discovery/) and/or [Product Strategy](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/pom/product-strategy/) discussions. Without a target audience, there are no problems to solve and no value to be created. That said - in the same way a "launch" is not a "success", the first target audience may not be the "best" target audience.  

There are many examples of teams in the world that adapted their target audiences once they realised that their initial approach did not bring the long-term business success they hoped for.
- Netflix initially targeted an older demographic used to DVDs before pivoting to a younger, tech-savvy audience
- Slack was an internal tool within a gaming company before it dramatically widened their audience
- YouTube started (and failed) as a dating service targeting young singles
- Starbucks used to target businesses as a wholesale coffee roasting company

When a product team has a clear understanding of who they're building for, they can tailor every aspect of the product to meet the specific needs, preferences, and pain points of that group. This focused approach leads to several key benefits: 
- It allows for **more efficient resource allocation**. Instead of spreading efforts thin trying to cater to everyone, teams can concentrate their time, budget, and expertise on solving problems for a specific group. This focused approach often results in a superior product experience for the chosen audience. 
- A well-defined target audience enables **more effective marketing and user acquisition strategies**. When a team knows exactly who they're trying to reach, they can craft messages that resonate deeply and choose channels that are most likely to reach their intended users. 
- Having a clear target audience facilitates **better decision-making throughout the product development process**. When faced with design choices or feature prioritisation decisions, teams can simply ask, "What would best serve our target audience?" This clarity can significantly speed up decision-making and reduce internal conflicts. 

On the other side, when product teams try to please too many different user personas simultaneously, they often fall into what's known as the "all things to all people" trap. This approach can lead to several significant problems: 
- It often results in a diluted product that doesn't excel in any area. By trying to accommodate the needs of diverse user groups, the product may end up with a **broad set of mediocre features** rather than a focused set of excellent ones. 
- It can lead to **confusing user experiences**. Different user groups often have different mental models and expectations. Trying to cater to all of these in a single product can result in an interface that's complex, cluttered, and difficult to navigate. 
- It makes marketing and positioning challenging. When a product doesn't have a clear target audience, it's **difficult to craft compelling marketing messages** or to decide which channels to focus on for user acquisition. 
- Trying to please everyone can lead to a **lack of product identity**. Products that stand out to users usually have a clear identity and a keen sense of "who they are". When this focus is missing, the product can struggle to differentiate itself from competitors. 

> Example: Consumer applications like Slack, Zoom or miro have become incredibly popular because they project a very clear product identity - this allows them to align all their efforts towards a clear value proposition.

By focusing on a well-defined group of users, teams can create products that truly resonate with their audience, achieve product-market fit, and stand out in crowded markets. While it may be tempting to try and appeal to a broad audience, the most successful products are often those that serve a specific group exceptionally well. 

## Strategies for Success Before Product-Market Fit 
Very few products reach product-market-fit early in their lifecycle. When working on an early product or an MVP, product teams can employ several strategies to mitigate risks and maximise the chances of success while working towards achieving product-market-fit. 

### Focus on Core Value Proposition 
Ensure that the product **always** delivers on its core value proposition as effectively as possible. Identify the one or two key features that are most critical to solving users' primary pain points and focus on making these as robust and user-friendly as possible. This approach can help create a positive initial impression even if other aspects of the product need refinement. 

### Adopt a Soft Launch 
Teams should consider proposing a soft launch approach. This strategy involves releasing the product to a limited audience and not promoting the product widely. This can provide valuable user data and minimise potential negative impacts. A soft launch allows the team to gather real-world usage data, identify critical issues, and make necessary improvements before a wider release. It also provides an opportunity to refine messaging and positioning based on initial user feedback. 

### Implement Robust Feedback Mechanisms 
During early stage it's even more important than normal to have strong systems in place for collecting and analysing user feedback. Teams can implement in-app feedback tools, conduct user surveys, and should closely monitor usage metrics. Detailed interview sessions with early users are often helpful to gain deeper insights into their experiences and needs. This focus on gathering feedback serves two purposes: it provides valuable data for improving the product, and it demonstrates to users that a team is committed to meeting their needs, which can help build goodwill even if the initial product doesn't yet solve all their needs. 

### Work with Realistic Expectations 
Realistic expectations for the initial launch help to prevent misunderstanding. Traditional criteria of *success* like user growth or high engagement are likely not attainable. Instead, the team should focus on learning objectives and improvement metrics. What key questions does it hope to answer with this launch? What improvements does it aim to make based on user feedback? By framing the launch as a learning opportunity rather than a key milestone, teams can alleviate pressure and create space for iteration and improvement. 

### Prepare for Rapid Iteration 
Early products should have the mindset and resources ready for rapid iteration. The development team must be prepared to quickly address critical issues or make necessary pivots based on user feedback. This might mean reserving capacity for bug fixes, setting up streamlined approval processes for changes, or even having a "war room" ready for the immediate post-launch period. 

### Develop a Clear Post-Launch Roadmap 
Besides fixing issues teams also should have a detailed plan for planned post-launch developments and improvements. This roadmap should be based on the current understanding of user needs and anticipated areas for improvement but should also be flexible enough to adapt based on actual user feedback post-launch. Teams should share this roadmap broadly to explain the strategic thinking and commitment to product success beyond the initial launch. 

#### Communicate Risks 
While not directly relevant to the product, it is very important to ensure that everyone in the organisation understands the risks associated with launching an early-stage product. Teams should explain the expected reactions and potential consequences of an early launch, such as negative user experiences, damage to reputation, or inefficient use of resources. Where possible, they should constructively propose strategies to mitigate risks and explain what they aim to learn from the release. 

By employing these strategies, product teams can navigate the challenges of launching before achieving product-market fit. While it's not an ideal scenario, with the right approach, it can become an opportunity for rapid learning and improvement, potentially accelerating the journey towards true product-market fit and long-term success. 

## When User Interests and Corporate Goals Collide 
It is a common situation that an existing business strategy does not fundamentally align with user needs. Users generally want things that cost money to build and run, businesses generally want to spend as little money as possible. This specifically occurs in times of economic difficulty, during downsizing, strategic re-platforming or when a group of users must change their way of working against their preferences for any reason.

> Example: A corporate decision has been made to replace the existing Salesforce CRM with an internally developed replacement. Many users have gotten used to Salesforce, they have voiced that they'd like to keep Salesforce and they don't see how the new solution can solve their needs. Because there is a significant saving to be made, the organisation has decided to replace Salesforce against the preference of their employees.

When the strategic goal of a team is misaligned with user interests in such a way, product-market-fit is extremely difficult to achieve. Since the team will still be expected to deliver on their strategic goals, it's important to approach the situation carefully (and ethically). Here's how a team can navigate such a difficult scenario. 

### Evaluate Feedback
The team should carefully listen to what is being said by users. Is the strategy really irreconcilable with user interests? Are users just hesitant to change? Is the value clearly communicated? Sometimes, what *appears* to be a conflict might have unexpected synergies or could be reframed in a way that aligns both corporate and user interests. The team can seek input from colleagues, conduct additional user research, or consult industry experts to see if a win-win scenario can be constructed.

> Example: In the example above, the team may find out that while Salesforce offers a lot of functionality, it is extremely slow and complex. By explaining to users that their tasks can be done in a lot less time, they may find a way to have users be less opposed to a change.

### Quantify the Misalignment 
If the team is confident in its assessment of a conflict, it should gather data to quantify the misalignment between the strategy and the known user needs. This might include user feedback, competitor analysis, or even financial projections showing the potential long-term consequences of pursuing the current strategy. Having concrete data will help with any ongoing conversations. 

### Develop Alternative Approaches 
Before making any decision, the team should try to develop one or more alternative strategies that could better align corporate goals with user needs. These alternatives should aim to satisfy the underlying business objectives while also creating value for users. The team must be prepared to explain how these alternatives could lead to better long-term outcomes for the company.

> Example: A viable option could be to replace only parts of Salesforce with an internal solution and leave other parts running as before.

### Escalate Concerns Appropriately 
To resolve a strategic conflict is often necessary to escalate the concerns to higher levels of leadership. This should be done thoughtfully and professionally, but firmly. The team should present its concerns along with supporting data and possible alternatives and frame the discussion in terms of long-term business success and risk mitigation rather than as criticism of current strategy. Ensure that the concerns and the data supporting them are well-documented. 

### Focus on Harm Mitigation 
If the strategy cannot be changed and user needs can't be met, the team should focus on mitigating potential harm to users. This might involve being extremely transparent about the product's limitations or potential downsides, prioritise the most impactful problems first, or finding ways to deliver some value to users even within the constraints of the conflicting strategy. 

### Maintain Professional Relationships 
Throughout this process, the team should strive to maintain professional relationships and open lines of communication. Its ability to influence outcomes over the long run often depends on the strength of the professional network and the respect the team and its various members have earned within the organisation. This type of reputation can be gained during difficult moments.

### Reflect and Learn from the Experience 
Regardless of the outcome, the team should treat the situation as a learning experience. Understanding how to navigate misalignments between corporate strategy and user needs is a valuable skill in product management. By reflecting on the situation and its actions, the team can take steps in the future to prevent and/or approach similar challenges in the future. 

Navigating a situation where corporate strategy seems fundamentally misaligned with user interests is one of the most challenging scenarios in product management. It requires a delicate balance of advocacy for users and respect for organisational goals. By approaching the situation thoughtfully and systematically, a team can maximise its chances of achieving the most positive outcome possible and be confident that they've done everything possible to advocate for users and long-term business success at the same time.

# Engagement Models
The way product teams interact with their users can significantly impact product adoption, user satisfaction, and overall success. This is particularly important for internal product teams, where the relationship with users often extends beyond the typical customer-vendor dynamic.

Engagement models define the interface between the product team and its users, determining how users learn about, adopt, and receive support for the product. Let's explore several common engagement models, along with their advantages and challenges.

## Self-Serve Model
In a self-serve model, users interact with the product independently, with minimal direct intervention from the product team. Self-serve models work best for products that are intuitive to use, have a high volume of users and low product complexity. In some cases, they can also be helpful for target groups that are intimately familiar with the product or task to be done.

**Examples:** Consumer productivity apps like Evernote or Todoist, email services like Gmail for personal use, simple photo editing tools like Canva, personal finance tracking apps like Mint, music streaming services like Spotify, code versioning tools like GitHub, ...

### Pros and Cons

| Pro | Con |
|-----|-----|
| Highly scalable, can support a large user base efficiently | Requires significant effort to create extensive documentation and intuitive design |
| Very cost-effective overall and at high user volumes | May lead to lower engagement if users struggle to understand the product |
| Empowers users to learn and explore at their own pace | Limited opportunity for the product team to gather direct user feedback |

## Scaled Support Model
This model provides a basic and scalable level of support to all users in addition to self-serve resources, typically through help centres, chatbots, or ticket-based systems. Scaled support works best for products that have somewhat higher product complexity or have some business-critical nature but are still relatively easy to understand and master.

**Examples:** E-commerce platforms like Shopify or WooCommerce, web hosting services like GoDaddy or Bluehost, customer relationship management (CRM) tools like HubSpot, project management software like Asana or Trello, social media scheduling tools like Buffer or Hootsuite, whiteboard software like miro, ...

### Pros and Cons

| Pro | Con |
|-----|-----|
| More supportive than purely self-serve, addressing more diverse user issues | May not be sufficient for complex use cases or enterprise clients |
| Still relatively scalable and cost-effective | Can lead to frustration if users feel their issues aren't adequately addressed |
| Can gather user feedback through support interactions | Requires ongoing maintenance of support resources |

## Guided Onboarding Model
In this model, new users receive structured guidance to help them get started with the product before being "handed over" to a self-serve or scaled support model. It is suitable for products that have a steeper learning curve and benefit from structured onboarding to ensure users can quickly realise their value.

**Examples:** Enterprise software like Salesforce or SAP, complex design tools like Adobe Creative Suite, data analysis platforms like Tableau or Power BI, marketing automation software like Marketo or Pardot, team collaboration tools like Microsoft Teams, ...

### Pros and Cons

| Pro | Con |
|-----|-----|
| Increases initial user engagement and adoption rates | Can be resource-intensive, especially for products with a large user base |
| Helps users quickly realise the product's value | May slow down the onboarding process for tech-savvy users |
| Opportunity to establish a positive relationship with users early on | Requires careful design to avoid overwhelming users with information |

## Solution Design Model
This is a very high-touch model that involves working closely with users to design customised solutions that fit their specific needs. This is suited for products that regularly require significant customisation and integration with existing systems, making a high-touch solution design approach beneficial.

**Examples:** Custom enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems, bespoke business intelligence solutions, large-scale cloud infrastructure services like AWS (Amazon Web Services) or Azure for enterprise clients, specialised scientific or engineering software, high-end cybersecurity solutions for large organisations, ...

### Pros and Cons

| Pro | Con |
|-----|-----|
| Highly effective for complex products or enterprise solutions | Not scalable for large user bases |
| Builds strong relationships with users | Resource-intensive and potentially costly |
| Provides deep insights into user needs and pain points | May lead to over-customization, making product maintenance challenging |

## Community-Based Model
This model leverages user communities for support, knowledge sharing, and engagement. These products benefit from active user communities that contribute to product development, provide peer support, and share knowledge.

**Examples:** Open-source software like Linux distributions or WordPress, developer tools and platforms like GitHub or Stack Overflow, massively multiplayer online games (MMOs), 3D modelling software like Blender, educational platforms like Coursera or edX

### Pros and Cons

| Pro | Con |
|-----|-----|
| Fosters a sense of belonging among users | Requires active community management to be effective |
| Can be highly scalable as users support each other | May lead to misinformation if not properly moderated |
| Provides a platform for gathering user feedback and ideas | Not suitable for products dealing with sensitive or confidential information |

## Tiered Engagement Model
This model offers different engagement models based on user needs or status, often combining elements of other models into a combination of service levels and support channels.

**Examples:** Any large product company that segments its users via pricing or other methods.

### Pros and Cons

| Pro | Con |
|-----|-----|
| Allows for efficient resource allocation, focusing high-touch engagement where it's most needed | Can be complex to manage and communicate to users |
| Can cater to a diverse user base with varying needs | May lead to perceptions of unfairness if not carefully implemented |
| Provides clear upgrade paths for users who need more support | Only works for products with considerable number of users and commercial segmentation |

## How to Choose a Suitable Model
For product teams, the choice of engagement model depends on factors such as the complexity of the product, the size and diversity of the user base, available financial resources, and organisational culture. Many mature products use a combination of models, adapting their approach based on user needs and product maturity. For small and early-stage products, it is often better to choose a single engagement model, ideally directly based on the chosen [target audience]().

When selecting an engagement model, product teams should consider the following factors:
- The technical proficiency of their user base
- The complexity and critical nature of the product
- Available resources within the product team
- Organisational expectations and culture
- The need for user feedback and co-creation
- Long-term scalability requirements

Choosing the appropriate engagement model is key decision to improve the chances of success for a product and the satisfaction of its users. The engagement model acts as the interface between the product team and the users, significantly impacting user adoption, retention, and overall product success.

Choosing the right model will ...
- ... ensure that users can effectively learn, adopt, and use the product, leading to higher satisfaction and retention.
- ... allow the product team to efficiently allocate their time and resources, focusing on areas that provide the most value.
- ... enable the product to scale effectively as the user base grows.
- ... create effective channels for gathering user feedback, vital for product improvement.
- ... balances the cost of supporting users with the value they derive from the product.

Choosing the wrong model might ...
- ... cause users to struggle to understand and implement advanced features, leading to underutilization of the platform's capabilities. This could result in users not realising the full value of the product.
- ... create frustrated users when trying to navigate complex setups or troubleshoot issues. This frustration could lead to negative perceptions of the product and potentially customer churn.
- ... cause the product team might miss opportunities to understand user needs and offer additional services or features that could benefit the user and increase revenue.
- ... limit the product team's ability to gather in-depth feedback about user needs and pain points, potentially slowing product improvement and innovation.
- ... create an increased support burden - paradoxically, a self-serve model for a complex product could lead to an increased burden on customer support as users struggle and seek help through whatever channels are available.

The key is to align the engagement model with the product's complexity, users' needs and expectations, and the business goals. Regular assessment and adjustment of the engagement model based on user feedback and product evolution is the key to long-term success.

# Working with sectors
Organisational design has a significant impact on how teams approach making their products successful. At MDAO, teams work within a so called hub-hub-spoke model, collaborating both with business divisions and end users. 

## What is a Hub-Hub-Spoke model?
Merck uses the concept of the hub-hub-spoke model, which describes a form of federated organizational structure. It is a form of decentralized management where a central authority shares responsibilities with semi-autonomous units. This model is often used in large, complex organizations to balance the benefits of centralized coordination with local autonomy and flexibility.

Key features of federated structures include:

Central Coordinating Body: 
- Sets overall strategy and policies
- Provides shared services and resources
- Ensures consistency across the organization

Semi-Autonomous Units:
- Have significant operational freedom
- Make decisions tailored to their specific context
- Retain their own identity and culture

Shared Governance:
- Units participate in decision-making at the central level
- Typically involves a system of representation

Resource Sharing:
- Units may share certain resources, knowledge, and best practices
- Central body often provides platforms and support functions

Standardization vs. Customization:
- Balance between organization-wide standards and local adaptations

Benefits of federated structures:
- **Flexibility:** Units can respond quickly to local needs and conditions
- **Innovation:** Encourages experimentation and diverse approaches
- **Risk management:** Failures in one unit are less likely to affect the whole
- **Engagement:** Can increase motivation and ownership at the unit level

Challenges:
- **Coordination:** Ensuring alignment across diverse units
- **Complexity:** Can be more difficult to manage than centralized structures
- **Duplicate efforts:** Risk of reinventing the wheel across units
- **Identity:** Balancing local and organizational identities

In this model at Merck, MDAO plays the role of the Central Coordinating Body. It delegates most of the responsibility of developing, implementing and supervising solutions to local hubs (the Sector Data Offices) while providing shared services and platforms.

## General Best practices
There are a few best practices for working as a product team in a central hub environment.

**Establish Clear Ownership:** Develop a clear governance structure that outlines roles, responsibilities, and decision-making processes across all levels of the model.

**Maintain communication:** Implement strong communication channels (e.g., weekly video conferences, forums, monthly reports) to ensure information flows smoothly between the central hub, local hubs, and spokes. The communication needs to flow in both directions.

**Implement Training and Development Programs:** Design and deliver training programs that transfer knowledge from the hubs to the spokes and back. This could include regular webinars, e-learning modules, and in-person training or shadow sessions. Focus on building both technical skills and the ability to apply this knowledge in business contexts. 

**Managing Change Effectively:** When implementing new initiatives or technologies, develop change management strategies that consider the needs and challenges at all levels of the organisation. 

**Focus your outlook on the future:** The role of the central hub should be forward-looking more than dealing with day-to-day operations. Use the central hub's position to guide the organisation's long-term technical strategy and prepare for future challenges, delegate as much of the day-to-day operations to the other hubs. 

## Building Relationships
As outlined above, one of the key challenges is coordinating a complex setup and handling larger complexity. BUilding strong relationships is one of the key mechanisms to achieve this. There are a few specific things that have proven valuable to build strong relationships across the different parts of an organisation. 

**Foster a Culture of Cross-Functional Collaboration:** Encourage cross-functional teams and projects that involve members from different hubs and spokes. This helps to break down silos and ensures that expertise is shared frequently across the organisation. 

**Create Cross-Functional Centers of Excellence:** Across hubs (and ideally spokes), establish centres of excellence for key technical areas (e.g. GenAI). These can be organised in the form of communities or chapters and serve as go-to resources for complex issues for these topics. This way any relevant information on the respective topics gets quickly distributed across the organisation. 

**Establish a "Product Champion" Network:** Identify key users across spokes who can act as liaisons between their teams and the product team, facilitating ongoing communication. These should be users that are both proficient and passionate about the product. Ensure these champions are connected with the local hubs. 

**Develop a Joint Onboarding Process:** Create a comprehensive program for new employees across all hubs and spokes to ensure that they understand the hub-hub-spoke model, their colleagues and their role within it. 

**Implement a Rotation Program:** Develop a program that allows employees to rotate between hubs and spokes. This can help spread knowledge, build relationships, and give employees a better understanding of the challenges of their colleagues. 

## Gathering Product Feedback
Gathering product feedback from a complex organisation can be challenging since complex organisations will often provide feedback of varying quality, with diverse points of view and even contradictory information. 

Below are a few things that can help to gather feedback from a diverse range of stakeholders as is common in a hub-hub-spoke setup. Not all of them should be used simultaneously and it depends on the organisation which ones are feasible. 

**Develop a Feedback Scoring System:** Create a transparent (and public!) rubric to evaluate information given to the product team based on factors like strategic alignment, [strength of evidence], potential impact, and feasibility. 

**Implement a Peer Review Process:** Have a panel of experienced users or stakeholders review and rate product feedback (can be combined with the above). This can be done as written information or in a conversational manner. 

**Use Comparative Scoring:** Ask users to rate new features or product changes in comparison to existing solutions or processes. This has the benefit of not only gathering feedback but also teaching requestors about the other things that the team is working on or thinking about. 

**Implement a Business Impact Assessment:** Require teams to estimate the business impact of their feedback in terms of time saved, cost reduction, or revenue generation. 

**Use frameworks:** Use established frameworks such as Jobs-To-Be-Done, Kano or RICE to structure and evaluate incoming product feedback. 

**Require Evidence-Backed Submissions:** For major feature requests or changes, require teams to provide supporting data or conduct small-scale pilots to quantify the potential impact. 

**Scenario Analysis:** Ask teams to provide best-case, worst-case, and most likely scenarios when describing the potential impact of their feedback. 

By focusing on these areas, teams can leverage the strengths of the hub-hub-spoke model while mitigating some of its potential challenges. Remember that successful implementation will require ongoing effort, regular evaluation, and a willingness to adapt as the organisation and its environment evolve.

# Balancing Complexity and Innovation
As digital products evolve and grow, they often accumulate features, integrations, and technical debt. This increasing complexity can lead to a situation where product teams find themselves spending too much time on operational and maintenance tasks, leaving little room for innovation and core product development. As a result, products stagnate and struggle to address the needs of their users.

The biggest danger here is that heavy operational loads often accumulate slowly and over time. Like the boiling frog paradigm, teams often realise their situation only when things have reached a critical level - at which point it is often difficult to rectify it.  

This section explores this common challenge and provides strategies to detect and mitigate the issue.

## Distinguishing Product and System Complexity
It is helpful to separate technical complexity into two buckets: System complexity and product complexity. While these are often related, they can behave independently of each other.

System complexity describes the technical complexity of the system itself, its code structure and architecture.

> Example: The same product can be created as a monolith or with a microservice architecture, in most cases, the microservice architecture will come with a significantly higher amount of technical complexity compared to the monolith.

Technical complexity manifests itself in tasks such as bug fixes, security patches, testing, QA, performance optimizations, deploying code, technical debt reduction and others.

Product complexity describes the complexity to run, maintain and administer the product itself. It manifests itself in tasks such as system monitoring, capacity planning, disaster recovery but also in day-to-day activities such as user support or data management.

In terms of requirements for a typical product team, either complexity draws resources. Technical complexity generally requires deeper technical expertise, while product complexity often involves a more operational component. In both cases the complexity has both an immediate impact (bugs, user access, ...) and a more ongoing, plannable impact (version updates, monitoring, testing, ...).

## Detecting the Complexity Burden
The first priority of any product team when it comes to managing complexity is to be aware of its situation. To do this, it is important to frequently conduct a time allocation analysis and assess how the team spends its time. This doesn't have to happen in a fully quantified manner, although teams can use existing developer tooling like Jira or Azure DevOps if it is already set up.  

If tasks related to managing complexity and maintaining status quo consistently consume more than a third of a product team's resources, it's a red flag that the team may be handling too much complexity.

The second priority is to catalogue any issues that require recurring maintenance and complexity management. This can be done via a few different channels:

- **Team-internal (e.g. sprint retrospectives):** A product team can use sprint retrospectives to identify recurring complexity issues. If the same problems keep surfacing, it's time to address the root causes.
- **Team-external (e.g. user feedback analysis):** Conversations with users can lead to information about product complexity.
- **Quantitative (e.g. technical debt metrics or ticket volumes):** A team can implement metrics to track technical and product complexity. Often used proxies are test coverage for technical complexity and analysis of incoming support requests for product complexity.

## Best Practices for Managing (too) High Complexity
In case a team deals with high levels of complexity, these are ways to handle and improve the situation.

- Declare an Emergency: If the levels are out of control, teams normally get overwhelmed. The first step is to officially declare an "emergency", raise awareness of the situation and assign clear ownership to for individuals to address distinct parts of the problem. This helps to create clear accountability.

- Make Room: The biggest issue for a team when faced with overwhelming complexity is to create room to handle it - everyone is already at full capacity. The team needs time to act, often this means that some *important* tasks will have to be postponed or even discarded.

- Get Help: In some cases, teams are so far *under water* that they won't be able to solve a complexity problem on their own. In this case it is essential for the team to get support from other parts of the organisation to solve the  situation.

- Create a Plan: Once the team has room to act it needs to set itself a realistic plan on how to get complexity to manageable levels. This can either be a one-time effort or ongoing - the details depend on the type of complexity at hand.

Once an immediate effort is in place to handle the complexity, the next step is to ensure that lower levels of complexity can be maintained in the future. Counterintuitively, growing the team rarely solves the problem long-term - the root causes lie elsewhere.

- **Rethink target audience(s):** Serving a diverse group of users often increases the complexity of a product significantly. In many cases, (temporarily) reducing the target audience can help a team to lower complexity to manageable levels. This can be done by segmenting the audience into priority groups or by excluding certain parts of the audience completely.

- **Rethink the feature set:** Develop a policy for sunsetting outdated features or components - and implement it. This can go along with a narrower focus from a reduced targe audience. Removing unnecessary elements can significantly reduce maintenance overhead.

- **Implement infrastructure:** Many complexity issues are common, so there are many vendors available that address some of these challenges. Implementing infrastructure such as disaster recovery, CI/CD or testing tools can help shift ongoing complexity.

- **Automate common tasks:** One effective way to handle unavoidable complexity is to automate tasks. This should only be done if it has been determined that the complexity cannot be removed and that the effort to automate is proportionate to the effort required to handle the task manually.

## Best Practices for Preventing Complexity
Strong product teams have processes in place that monitor the build-up and prevent high complexity levels. Here are a few best practices.

- **Carefully evaluate product changes:** The most important aspect of managing complexity is by keeping the product focused. Each new functionality should add a high amount of value relative to its development effort and the added long-term complexity. Ideally, new complexity is only introduced when other parts of the system can be simplified.

- **Modern Engineering Practices:** Strong technical teams have various processes and techniques in place to prevent complexity from building up. These range from modal architecture to code reviews, strong testing regimes, QA processes and continuous refactoring. Patterns like Strangler Fig are good starting points.

- **Implement a Technical Debt Budget:** Many teams allocate a specific percentage of each sprint or development cycle to addressing technical debt and add automation. This prevents it from accumulating to unmanageable levels.

- **Feature Flags:** The use of feature flags can help to gradually roll out new features but also to offer features to a specific audience only to prevent product complexity. This approach allows for easier rollback if issues arise and does not expose high complexity features to a wide range of users.

- **Monitoring and Logging:** High complexity often causes troubleshooting to take a long time. Robust monitoring and logging systems can help quickly identify and diagnose issues, reducing the time spent on maintenance tasks.

- **Leverage Managed Services:** The use of 3rd party services and managed solutions offers a trade-off between complexity and customisation. Using external solutions shifts the handling of complexity to service providers but trades off fine-grained control over the functionalities provided.  

By implementing these strategies, product teams can more effectively manage complexity and maintain sufficient capacity to innovate and evolve the product.

# Empowered Product Teams 

Embracing the empowered product team model is an exciting opportunity. An "empowered product team" in the context of Product Management refers to a group of professionals who have the authority, skills, resources, and accountability to manage a product. 

These teams are tasked with defining and achieving the product's outcomes based on a broad problem statement and strategic context, and they possess a significant degree of autonomy in decision-making regarding the product's features, design, and execution. The core philosophy behind empowered product teams is to foster innovation and rapid iteration by giving the team direct ownership of their product's success, rather than merely executing predefined plans.

# What makes empowered product teams different? 
Empowered product teams differ in some specific ways from other teams. 

![image_1](https://docs.uptimize.merckgroup.com/docs-assets/pom/empowered_vs_traditional.PNG)

## Autonomy in Execution 
The biggest difference to other team structures is that empowered teams have considerable autonomy. They should be given broad problems to solve instead of narrow instructions to execute. 

> Example: Consider a team responsible for improving adoption of a feature. In the traditional setup a manager or business staleholder would tell the technical team **what** tasks to do, while an empowered team would get guidance that improved adoption is important and needs to find ways **how** to achieve this. 

Working with broad problem statements allows empowered teams to autonomously determine the best ways to achieve the desired outcomes through their strategies and day-to-day decisions. This autonomy allows for more innovative approaches to problem-solving and product development, as teams are not strictly bound by rigid plans but can adapt based on real-world feedback and changing market conditions. 

> In the above example, a team may realise that many users that sign up for the product are not getting value from it, which causes poor adoption. Refining the product positioning and target audience may have a bigger impact than spending a lot of time on the onboarding funnel. 

## Enhanced Responsibility and Accountability 
As is widely known: With great power comes great responsibility.

Empowered teams are given the autonomy and authority to make wide-ranging decisions. This only works if the teams are held accountable for the outcomes of their actions and decisions. This heightened sense of responsibility has both positive and challenging aspects.

On the positive side, it encourages team members to be more invested in the product's success. It fosters a sense of ownership that can lead to higher motivation and more thoughtful decision-making, directly impacting the quality and success of the product. 

On the challenging side, responsibility requires the team to take ownership and accountability for the ultimate result. It is not enough to just deliver a specific request or feature, it is important to deliver the outcome (business value) that is expected. 

## Acceptance of things going wrong 
The above directly leads to another key differentiating factor. Traditional teams are often used to avoiding "failures". A lot of work goes into delivering projects on-time, in-budget or with the idea that the launch should be "perfect". This approach is often coupled with the traditional perception that specific instructions (given from above) represent the "optimal" solution and execution path. 

Empowered teams assume that every decision is made with incomplete information. They embrace this reality and accept that they will make bad decisions - they understand that handling bad decisions is much more important than avoiding them. This allows them to be more flexible and adapt to changing environments much faster than traditional teams that focus on avoiding mistakes that are empirically unavoidable. 

## Cross-functional in Nature 
The last thing that makes empowered teams different is that they need to have a comprehensive understanding of the problem and solution spaces. This requires a broad set of skills and expertise in the team, and it also assumes a larger amount of equality between different team members. 

This cross-functionality shows itself both directly in empowered teams (often referred to as the so-called "trio" of design, product and engineering) and through close collaboration with adjacent teams (e.g. marketing, governance, IT infrastructure, business stakeholders, legal, finance, ...). As an example, strong Product Managers should have a very good understanding of unit economics or legal restrictions for their product.

Being cross-functional has two main benefits: The team itself becomes better at developing solutions when it has a broad cross-functional view on things. And since their problems are broad, the information they need is broad as well. They can use adjacent teams to understand what information the team needs and which problem they are trying to solve - this creates a better understanding of what they can expect in terms of functionality.

# What does it mean for leaders? 
The most important thing to remember with empowered product teams is that the core ideas behind them are autonomy and decentralisation. This is different to traditional projects or IT structures where management is often understood as giving instructions to be followed. 

Leaders coming from traditional structures often need to adapt their leadership style. As leaders they have an outsized effect on their teams' success, adapting leadership to the concept of empowerment of product teams is a critical step to be successful. Leadership in an organisation made up of empowered product teams must be based on fostering a trusted, collaborative, and aligned environment, in which the various teams have the tools, confidence and strategic guidance to make their own decisions. 

The following concepts are best practice when leading empowered product teams. 

## Support and Encourage 
Working in an empowered team can be tough, especially in the beginning and during times of transition. Empowered teams need more encouragement, support and - occasionally - protection than traditional teams due to the bigger responsibility they have (this is true for all members, not just the ones that have "product" in the title). Leaders need to be aware that team members not used to empowered teams will work outside their comfort zone frequently and be ready to jump in and support them where needed. 

An extremely important aspect is how "failure" is being treated - it is common for empowered teams to experiment with multiple workable solutions before coming to one that adds the most value. The solutions that did not work out should not be seen as failures but as learnings along the way to the best solution. Instead of feeling uncomfortable with trying out things and "fail", it is important for the team to embrace experimentation and pivoting between different solutions. Leaders must help and support their teams in treating "failures" as part of the normal operating model - helping team members to manage them instead of trying to avoid them. Depending on the performance management practices, leaders may have to adapt incentive systems. 

## Guide and Delegate
Allowing an empowered team to operate autonomously requires leaders to step back and focus on guidance and delegation over active participation on a day-to-day level. This can be hard for leaders that are used to a more directive leadership style (and it can also overwhelm team members that aren't used to it). It is important to gradually adopt such a style of leadership when transitioning teams into empowered product teams. 

Well-structured guidance allows teams to make better decisions and align their decisions with the rest of the organisation. Empowered teams need a clear understanding about the bigger picture to make the best use of their freedom to operate on the smaller day-to-day activities. Strong leaders focus on overall guidance. 

> Example: Consider a team responsible for developing data pipelines. In their day-to-day work they will come across conflicting requests from stakeholders. Some want speed, some want reliability, some want customization, some want to save costs. As a team, it is important to have guidance on what the overall organisation values most - this is a key input for their decision making. The role of the leader is to help the team understand what the organisation values most - weak guidance such as "make better pipelines" is not sufficient. A better way to guide a team would be *"The highest priority of the organisation is to make fast pipelines. We do expect certain minimum bars on reliability. Outside of that, you can apply common sense."* 

Delegation is a key leadership concept when collaborating with empowered teams and embracing delegation is essential for fostering a positive and impactful work environment. Effective delegation aligns with the fundamental principles of empowered teams such as accountability and encourages team members to take ownership of their work, make decisions, and learn from their experiences.

By delegating responsibility, leaders empower their teams to take charge and drive the success of their products and components, contributing to a more resilient and product-centric operating model. 

## Team structure
There are two areas where leaders can not delegate and have to take explicit responsibility - that is setting up the scope of the team and managing performance.

For a team to feel autonomous, their scope needs to be cut in a way that there is actual autonomy. Real autonomy means that a team has meaningful amount of authority over achieving outcomes and technical aspects on which technology to use and how to use it. If every decision requires multiple other teams  for approvals and business stakeholders dictating outcomes and output, the basis for empowerment is missing. It is the role of leadership to make sure that each team has a scope and environment that allows autonomous decision making. 

Extending this thought in the context of transformation processes, leaders are also required to assess to what degree existing teams can realistically become *fully* empowered. Sometimes a re-scoping may be necessary for a transformation to be successful. For certain periods of a transformation, it can also be helpful to mix traditional and empowered teams if the current skill sets of team members temporarily puts restrictions on forming empowered teams. 

## Connect the dots
Empowered teams don't operate in isolation. Leaders of empowered product teams are responsible for connecting with other leaders and aligning the work of different teams to make sure that the guidance given to each team creates an overall meaningful picture.  

> Example: Continuing from above, the guidance to focus on pipeline speed must be aligned with the rest of the organisation. If teams at the receiving end of the pipelines assume that pipelines are primarily reliable, it is likely that there will be friction since there are competing assumptions at play. 

In addition to giving guidance to outline broad directional pathways for their own teams to follow, leaders must also work horizontally and upwards to ensure that multiple empowered teams are pulling towards the same bigger goal. 

## DANGER: Command-and-control anti-pattern 
The most common anti-pattern for leaders in empowered teams scenarios is that they fail to let go of a control-and-command leadership style. This causes various problems: 

**Product Status Quo:** Command-and-control management stifles creativity and innovation because it relies heavily on hierarchy and rigid structures. This management style implicitly assumes that senior leaders "know best", which overlooks valuable insights from team members closer to the problem or market. 
**Demotivated Individuals:** In a command-and-control environment, teams lack autonomy and ownership over their work, which can diminish their motivation and commitment to the product's success. When teams are merely executing orders, they are less likely to be engaged or to push boundaries.

# What does it mean for team members? 
Working in an empowered team is generally more fulfilling than working in a traditional team, but it also asks more of each team member, especially the more senior ones.

In empowered teams, members generally take more responsibility while dealing with more complex problem spaces. They need to be able to navigate ambiguity, master a broader set of skills and resolve more conflicts. In traditional team setups these tasks are often taken care of by "the management", leaving team members with less complexity to handle.  

As a result, team members often feel a stronger sense of purpose and commitment, since they are responsibly for the overall outcome of their work and they have a much bigger agency when it comes to making choices on how to achieve their goals.

## Broader responsibilities 
Members of empowered teams are not tasked with executing a given task, they are asked to actively participate in shaping the overall success of the product and the team. This requires them to have a broader understanding of the overall problem space and go beyond their specific expertise.

> Example: It is common for Product Managers from empowered teams to participate in or even drive commercial negotiations with customers. 

## Look beyond their own role 
Especially senior members of empowered teams wear more hats than in traditional hierarchical setups. The effect of this is that members of empowered teams often cultivate a more generalist set of skills. Engineers are included in product discussions, product people become business experts, business stakeholders develop strong technical understanding of the product, etc.

> Example: When senior engineers develop a sense of economic realities, they often make more informed technical decisions since they know the financial limitations the team operates in.

## Managing ambiguity 
Empowered teams receive a lot fewer specific instructions. Instead of executing pre-defined plans, they are much more often included in the planning process itself and asked to contribute to decision making. This can cause anxiety and stress for team members that are not used to making decisions or working in scenarios without clear instructions. 

> Example: In traditional delivery organisations, a single person is often responsible for defining specs and *definitions of done* for all engineering tasks while multiple engineers are responsible for implementing the specs. In empowered teams, individual engineers are given a lot more ownership over their individual task, which requires them to develop a deeper understanding of the business value that they are unlocking. 

## Continuous learning
The existence of ambiguity and the broader scope highlights the need for continuous learning. As team members are being asked to contribute beyond their traditional scope, they are expected to learn about topics both close to their core function as well as adjacent functions.

> Example: When a team starts working on a product that deals with specific regulatory restrictions, it is helpful for all team members to develop a basic sense of what these restrictions entail and how that influences the work of the team as a whole.

## Resolve differences in opinion 
In traditional team setups conflicts are often resolved by escalating them to a manager or senior leader, even on a day-to-day level. Empowered teams are expected to resolve conflicts internally.

This requires strong skills when it comes to conflict resolution itself but also the ability of everyone on the team to empathise with the other team members and hear out differing opinions. Finally it requires clear responsibilities and processes inside the team to handle such discussions.

# What does it mean for the day-to-day? 
In theory, empowered teams are relatively straightforward to understand. On a day-to-day basis is it not always obvious on how to operationalize an environment with empowered teams.

Here are the main guidelines to follow.

## Strong Principles Versus Rigid Rules 
The most important thing to remember with empowered product teams is that the core idea behind them is decentralisation. A decentralised organisation thrives on autonomy and flexibility. Autonomy works best when strong, consistent guiding principles serve as the foundational compass. This enables members to navigate and innovate while adhering to a set of widely accepted principles. 

Principles provide a shared understanding and collective commitment. They bind teams together while allowing each team to operate independently and efficiently. It creates a sense of purpose and direction without the need for constant oversight, enabling the organisation to thrive in a dynamic environment. This fosters a culture of trust and innovation. Merck has developed [five principles](link) when it comes to operating with empowered teams.

This aligns with concepts from information technology such as microservices in software engineering or data meshes in analytics. Common to all of these ideas is that - instead of a rigid monolith with strong rules on naming and data structures - the solution is a loose coupling of semi-autonomous units that have a strong internal cohesion. This means that each unit can move, change and develop on its own (autonomous) - as long as it maintains a few, clearly defined interfaces (principles) to the other units in the system (loosely coupled). 

In comparison, employing strong rules can impose a rigidity that contradicts the essence of decentralisation. Rules are prescriptive, detailing specific actions and responses, which can stifle creativity and adaptability as they enforce a uniform way of acting across teams. A decentralised organisation must avoid a bureaucratic structure where compliance overshadows growth and evolution.

## Communication is critical 
Building an organisation on principles rather than rules requires significantly more communication. Most organisations that use empowered product teams **under-communicate, especially verbally and in-person**. 

This necessity arises from the nature of principles, which are broad and provide general guidelines rather than specific directives. While this allows teams to be fast and make autonomous decisions, employees need to understand how to interpret and apply these principles in various situations, making context-specific guidance essential. 

- **Vertical communication:** Leaders and managers must frequently engage with their teams in vertical communication to ensure everyone grasps the intended meaning and application of these principles across different contexts. 
- **Horizontal communication:** Since teams are semi-autonomous and adapt quickly, larger initiatives involving multiple teams require more on-going alignment, which is best done through communication rather than rigid planning instruments. This way the frequent changes and adaptations at the detail level can be discussed and - where appropriate - changes can be made to the broader context. 

The culture within the organisation also benefits from regular communication. Reinforcing the organisation's culture involves consistently linking actions and decisions back to its core principles. Sharing stories and examples of how principles are applied in practice helps embed these values into the organisational culture, akin to how empowered teams share their successes and learnings to strengthen their collaborative spirit. 

In comparison, an organisation built on rules will require less frequent communication since rules are specific and leave little room for interpretation. This often shows in day-to-day operations, verbal, in-person communication is often described as 'overhead' and 'wasted time'. However, cutting back on communication and relying on strict planning leads to rigidity and a lack of adaptability. Often this setup leads to a scenario called the **"Build Trap"**, where teams follow predefined plans that they subconsciously know are flawed, but the rigid rules dictate their actions. 

## Team composition matters 
Getting team composition right is important to successfully operate with empowered teams. As a rule of thumb, empowered teams are more diverse and more complex than traditional teams and work together over a longer period. 

- **Core composition:** At its core, an empowered product team should have a product person, a product designer and one or more engineers on the team. Depending on size, lots of other roles can be added. It is also common that certain roles are shared across multiple empowered teams (such as user research). 
- **Long-term membership:** Empowered product teams benefit from their members being with the team for extended periods of time (1.5+ years). Giving a team autonomy and accountability requires team members to have strong and broad domain expertise, which takes time to build. Swapping team members on a quarterly or semi-annual basis is an anti-pattern that should be strictly avoided.
- **Cross-functional:** Empowered teams often add expertise from outside to the team, sometimes temporary and sometimes permanent. For example, it is common for empowered teams that deal with complex products to add dedicated product success expertise to the team, e.g. from after-sales or solution engineering departments. Other examples can be business analysts or lawyers or compliance specialists (also see below).

Another key characteristic of empower teams is that they are fluid when it comes to responsibilities inside the team. Due to their relatively small size compared to their broad scope, they rarely have dedicated specialists for all activities. This results team members learning beyond their specific roles and take on responsibilities often not part of the "classic" role they have. For example, UI designers perform user research, product managers often double up as data analysts and software engineers regularly manage backlogs and help define requirements. 

## Gaining trust from stakeholders 
A big question in most organisations (especially larger ones) is how empowered product teams interface with other parts of the business. Since they have a lot of autonomy over their work, the question of who "controls" what is being developed is often a contentious topic. 

For example, in B2B contexts, sales teams generally want to influence product strategy and decision making to have better chances to sign important deals. In B2C contexts it is often marketing or customer service/support teams that have strong opinions and in internal setups the pressure comes from business units or individual executives. 

The best way to handle such situations is to gain trust by relying on the principle of cross-functionality. Being transparent and involving distinct parts of the organisation in decision making often helps to create strong bonds with stakeholders. In many situations it can be extremely advantageous to add members from other teams to the empowered product team temporarily. A couple of examples: 
- **Key Account, Delivery or Customer Success:** In a B2B context, important customers often have dedicated points of contact in the business. These employees generally have extremely valuable insight into the customer ("Mr Smith has never made a reasonable request ever..."). Including them in discussions that are relevant for their customers can lead to vastly improved outcomes. 
- **Legal, Security, Privacy, Compliance:** In certain situations, staying within the limits of what is allowed is more important than squeezing out the last bit of revenue or technical performance. Adding representatives from the relevant teams to the product team (even just temporarily) can foster a sense of "joint effort" that can be beneficial. 
- **Marketing, Sales:** For products that rely on manual efforts to sell it can help enormously to add a member of the teams that will do the work to the product team. Not only will they understand the technical realities much better ("sure it can't be that difficult to add a button down there, right?"), they will also point out domain-specific issues that are really hard for the product to even consider ("Sorry, the icons you are using here are very offensive in the Chinese market, which is one of biggest"). 
- **Solution Enablers:** Especially for platform products, the real work to create value often happens after a product is built and/or sold. Prominent examples are platforms like SAP, which are essentially useless unless a trained professional implements them in the context of the organisation. Having the employees that drive the implementation strongly associated with the product team can lead to drastically better decisions, especially when it comes to assessing impact and value creation. 

## Setting value-based objectives - outcomes over output 
Setting goals for empowered product teams can be vastly different to traditional project or IT structures where success is often measured by how well a certain set of requirements was implemented, but not what happened after the deliverables were finished.  

In comparison, the objectives for empowered product teams often centre around product operations or product usage, which goes well beyond the physical implementation of the solution. This can cause a significant amount of stress and anxiety, both at team and at management level - since influencing the behaviour of users is not something that can be achieved by purely implementing a technical solution. A mindset shift is required for everyone involved to make this a success. 

The best practice for setting objectives and incentives is to apply a mix of several types of results that reflect the purpose of the team. This can differ quite significantly from team to team; deeply technical backend and platform teams may have a different focus than product teams that work primarily with consumer products. Common to all should be an emphasis on outcomes rather than outputs, a focus on customer value, and ideally a nod to innovation and continuous improvement.  

Here are some examples of goals an empowered product team might focus on. This list notably does not include objectives common to project or IT organisation such as on-time or in-budget delivery, since the focus is not on the output of the team (what was delivered) but on the outcome (what result was achieved). At the same time, not all example goals are restricted to immediate value creation - teams will also have intermediate goals that enable value creation in the future. 

### Customer-Centric Goals 
- User Satisfaction: Aim to increase customer satisfaction scores through enhancements in usability, features, and overall user experience. 
- User Adoption: Make sure that available functionalities are found and used by users of the product. 
- User Engagement: Increase user engagement metrics such as usage frequency, session length, or specific interactions with the product. 

### Business Impact Goals 
- Revenue Growth: Focus on increasing revenue associated with the product, whether through expanding the user base, enhancing monetization strategies, or improving retention. 
- Market Expansion: Enter new markets or demographic segments, aiming to broaden the product's reach and impact. 
- Cost Reduction: Identify and implement efficiencies that reduce costs without compromising product quality, such as optimizing resource usage or streamlining processes. 

### Technical Goals 
- Quality Improvement: Reduce bugs and errors, improve system stability, and enhance performance metrics. 
- Security Enhancements: Increase the security level of the product, aiming to protect user data and reduce vulnerabilities. 
- Evolve Technologies: Retire outdated technologies and upgrade to more appropriate versions. Trial innovative technologies that can improve product offerings or user experience, such as AI, machine learning, or blockchain. 

### Team-Internal Goals 
- Speed to Market: Improve the time it takes to go from idea to deployment, enhancing the team's agility and responsiveness to market changes. 
- Process Optimization: Streamline processes to increase efficiency, such as improving the CI/CD pipeline for faster and more reliable deployments. 
- Team Development: Focus on skill development and cross-functionality within the team to ensure all members can contribute effectively. 
- Collaboration and Communication: Enhance collaboration both within the team and with external stakeholders to ensure transparency and alignment on objectives. 

Regardless of which goals are chosen, it's crucial that they are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound ([SMART](link)).

# Metrics and Performance
To effectively measure the performance of an empowered product team, teams can look at a variety of metrics and indicators that reflect both the efficiency of the team and the success of the product in the market. Here are key performance indicators (KPIs) to consider, as well as signs that might indicate a team is not performing well.

## 1. Product Metrics
Ultimately, the success of an empowered product team should result in a successful product. Below are some common success metrics. The most basic indicator is that the metrics themselves exist. The more they exist and the more they are used on a regular basis, the more competent the team normally is.

* User Growth: Increasing numbers of new users or subscribers.
* Engagement: High and improving levels of user interaction with the product.
* Retention Rates: High percentage of users continuing to use the product over time.
* Revenue Metrics: Increasing revenue, achieving, or exceeding monetization goals.
* Customer Feedback: Positive feedback and low rates of complaints or negative reviews.
* Usability Scores: High scores in usability tests, suggesting the product is user-friendly.

## 2. Team Health Metrics
Specific internal team metrics can also be used. These often allow a deeper understanding on what exactly happens inside the team.

* Employee Satisfaction: Satisfaction rates within the team, e.g., assessed through regular surveys.
* Psychological Safety: The willingness of team members to communicate openly and make themselves vulnerable to criticism
* Low Turnover Rates: Stability in team composition, with few unplanned departures.
* Innovation and Experimentation: Regular introduction of innovative ideas and approaches, and a high ratio of experiments to ideas.

## 3. Cross-functional Metrics
Strong empowered product teams have strong relationships and elevated levels of trust within their organisation. They build this over time by being reliable, valuable partners and good colleagues. This can be measured by their overall reputation.

* Quality of results: The number of times a team has delivered strong results in the past
* Quality of communication: The amount of information the team shares and how well they are able to communicate this information.
* Thought Leadership: The number of times that other parts of the organisation come to the empowered product team for advice or guidance.
* Citizenship: A measure of how much the team gives back to the overall community versus only focusing on their own success.

## 4. Operational Metrics
While occasionally helpful, operational metrics should be taken with a grain of salt. Teams that follow the feature factory pattern often have strong operational metrics but deliver little value. That said, consistently bad metrics in this area indicate a concern on the performance of an empowered product team.

* Velocity: Speed at which new features are developed and released.
* Quality Metrics: Low bug rates, high quality of releases (measured by fewer critical bugs or downtime post-launch).
* Cycle Time: High rate of work completed on schedule.
* SLAs and Support: The time it takes for users to receive support in case they run into issues

Monitoring these indicators should be an ongoing process, and addressing any negative trends early can help prevent larger issues. Regularly reviewing these metrics and adjusting strategies or processes accordingly can significantly enhance both the team's effectiveness and the product's success in the market.

# Product Mindset
In digital product development, success hinges as much on technical skills and user insights, as on the right mindset. The Product Mindset is a holistic set of principles that empowers teams to create innovative, user-centric solutions while navigating the complexities of modern business environments. 

At the heart of the Product Mindset lies a set of interconnected principles that guide decision-making and foster a culture of continuous improvement. These principles include user centricity, focusing on outcomes rather than outputs, data-driven decision making, and embracing accountability and ownership. Each of these elements plays a distinct and significant role in shaping how product teams approach challenges, interact with users, and deliver value. 

The Product Mindset also emphasises the importance of flexibility and adaptability. Concepts such as "strong opinions, loosely held" and maintaining a growth mindset enable teams to make decisive moves while remaining open to additional information and learning from both successes and failures. This balance is essential in today's fast-paced, ever-changing product landscape. 

By understanding and applying these principles, product teams can create an environment that fosters innovation, enhances collaboration, and ultimately leads to the development of products that truly meet user needs and drive business success.

# Principles instead of Rules
One of the core foundations in the product mindset is the paradigm to rely on strong principles instead of rules and processes. This is because principles are generally more valuable than rigid rules and processes in organisations dealing with ambiguity and change for several key reasons: 

**Flexibility:** Principles provide guidance while allowing adaptability to different situations. This is beneficial when facing unpredictable challenges or rapid changes. 

**Decision-making:** They offer a consistent basis for making decisions, even in novel situations where specific rules might not exist. 

**Empowerment:** Principles enable employees to use their judgement and expertise, fostering innovation and problem-solving. 

**Scalability:** As organisations grow or evolve, principles remain relevant, whereas rigid rules may become obsolete or require constant updating. 

**Cultural alignment:** Principles help create a shared understanding of organisational values, promoting cohesion even as specific practices change. 

**Long-term focus:** They emphasise the "why" behind actions, encouraging thoughtful decisions aligned with long-term goals rather than short-term compliance. 

**Simplicity:** A set of core principles is often easier to remember and apply than a complex rulebook, especially in fast-paced environments.

# User Centricity 
User centricity is a fundamental principle in digital product management that places the user's needs,  pain points, and experiences at the core of all product decisions and development processes. User centricity means designing and developing products with the primary goal of solving real user problems and meeting their genuine needs, rather than focusing primarily on technology or business objectives. 

The key aspects of user centricity are research, empathy and ongoing product feedback. Conducting thorough user research helps to identify tactical pain points, desires, and behaviours. Empathy helps product teams to develop a deep understanding of users' contexts, motivations, and challenges. There are many ways to bring this principle to life, for example through user personas, user journeys and UX design and testing. 

When done correctly, being user-centric can create increased user satisfaction and loyalty, higher adoption and retention rates and more efficient development (building what users need). Things to look out for during implementation is balancing user needs with business goals (not every need can be fulfilled), managing diverse user groups with diverse needs (choosing target audiences is critical) and avoiding bias in user research and interpretation (expert advice and training can help). 

> Note: One important and sometimes misunderstood facet of user centricity is that the goal is not to build what users say or ask for. The goal is to build what solves their problems. In many cases, the best (and most viable) way to solve a user's problem may not be known to them. This is why it is critical to focus user conversations on problems, not on solutions. It is then the task of the product team to come up with ideas for solutions.

# Focus on Outcomes 
Understanding the distinction between outcome and output and focusing on the former is at the heart of effective product management and can significantly influence a team's approach and success. 

**Output** refers to the tangible deliverables that a team produces. This often includes features, functionalities, data pipelines, lines of code, or even entire products. Outputs are typically easy to measure and quantify. For instance, a team might release a new user interface, implement a search function, or launch a mobile app. These are all concrete, observable results of the team's work. 

**Outcomes**, on the other hand, focuses on the actual impact or value that these outputs create for users and the business. Outcomes are about the change in user behaviour, the problems solved, or the benefits realised because of the team's work. They're often more challenging to measure but are ultimately what determines the success of a product. An outcome might be increased user engagement, higher customer retention, improved productivity, or enhanced user satisfaction. 

The key difference lies in their relationship to value creation. While outputs are necessary steps in the development process, they don't inherently guarantee value. An elegantly coded feature that users don't need or want is still an output, but it doesn't lead to a positive outcome. Conversely, a simple change that significantly improves user experience could be a small output but lead to a substantial outcome in terms of user satisfaction and product adoption. 

In practice, this distinction shapes how teams approach their work. Output-focused teams often **prioritise shipping features quickly or meeting predefined specifications**. They might measure success by the number of features released or story points completed. While this can lead to a sense of productivity, it doesn't necessarily translate to user or business value. 

Outcome-focused teams, however, **start with the desired impact and work backwards**. They ask questions like "What problem are we solving?" or "How will this change user behaviour?" before deciding what to build. They often ship fewer features, but each one is carefully considered in terms of its potential impact. These teams measure success not by what they produce, but by the change it creates in the world. 

It is important to note that outputs and outcomes aren't mutually exclusive. Outputs are often the means to achieve outcomes. The key is to maintain a clear focus on outcomes while planning and executing outputs. This balance allows teams to stay grounded in day-to-day development work while keeping sight of the bigger picture.

# Act as the owner and accept accountability 
Strong product teams are characterised by a deep-seated sense of ownership over their product's success. This ownership mentality isn't limited to the product itself, but extends to its impact on users, the business, and even the broader market. It's a mindset that permeates every aspect of their work, from strategic planning to day-to-day operations.  

## Setting Clear, Ambitious Goals
At the heart of ownership is the establishment of clear, measurable, and ambitious goals. These aren't vague aspirations, but specific, time-bound objectives derived from the overall company strategy that challenge the team to push beyond their comfort zones. For instance, a team might aim to increase user retention by 25% within six months or to become the top-rated analytics destination in their company within a year. These goals serve as a north star, guiding decisions and priorities. MyGPT Suite can be a great companion in writing SMART goals. 

## Personal Commitment to Outcomes
What sets high-performing teams apart is that this sense of ownership isn't limited to leadership or product  owners. Every team member, regardless of their role - be it designer, developer, researcher, or data analyst - feels personally invested in these outcomes. They understand how their individual contributions tie into the larger goals and are committed to the product's success as if it were their own business. 

## Breaking Boundaries 
Ownership often means pushing beyond the confines of traditional role boundaries. A developer might take the initiative to speak directly with users to understand their pain points on the specific functionality he is working on. A designer might dive into analytics to gauge the impact of their latest interface changes. This boundary-spanning behaviour ensures that no aspect of the product's success falls through the cracks. 

## Proactive Problem-Solving 
An essential aspect of ownership is proactivity, especially when faced with challenges. When confronted with issues like unexpectedly low adoption of a new feature, teams with strong ownership don't wait for directives from above. Instead, they  take action, demonstrating initiative and resourcefulness. 

## Accountability and Learning
Ownership also means taking responsibility for both successes and setbacks. When goals are met, the team celebrates collectively. When they fall short, there's no finger-pointing. Instead, the team views it as an opportunity for learning and improvement. They conduct thorough  retrospectives, identifying lessons that can be applied to future initiatives. 

## Long-Term Perspective 
Teams with strong ownership think beyond short-term wins. They consider the long-term health of the product and its ecosystem. This might mean advocating for technical debt reduction even when it doesn't have immediate user-facing benefits, or pushing for foundational changes that will enable future innovations. 

## Influencing the Broader Organization 
Ownership often extends beyond the immediate product team. These teams become product advocates within the broader organisation, influencing company strategy, securing resources, and building cross-departmental alliances to support their product's success. 

For product leaders, fostering this sense of ownership is a key responsibility. They can promote ownership by: 
1. Empowering team members to make decisions 
2. Providing transparency into product strategy and performance 
3. Recognizing and rewarding initiative 
4. Creating a safe environment for experimentation and learning from failure 
5. Aligning individual growth with product success 

Taking ownership in product management is about creating a culture where every team member feels deeply responsible for the product's success. It's characterised by clear goal setting, personal investment, proactive problem-solving, and a willingness to go beyond role boundaries. When cultivated effectively, this ownership mentality can be a powerful driver of innovation, user satisfaction, and business success.

# Data-driven Decision Making
Teams that embrace data-driven decision making significantly improve their chances of success. This approach provides a robust foundation for transparent, justifiable, and effective product development. 

The cornerstone of data-driven decision making is a well-structured analytics framework. This goes beyond basic metrics and involves creating a holistic view of the product ecosystem. Strong product teams invest time and resources in setting up a comprehensive system that tracks information like user engagement, task completion rates, team productivity, customer satisfaction, business impact, and technical performance. This multifaceted approach provides a **360-degree view of the product's performance and impact**, enabling teams to make informed decisions based on a broad range of data points. 

When faced with important decisions, such as introducing new features or making significant changes to the product, data-driven teams should follow a **structured approach**. They begin by forming clear hypotheses about the expected impact of the change. This is followed by data collection from as many sources as reasonable, including historical product data, user research, and technical performance. Teams can also delve deep into user behaviour analysis, utilising tools like heat maps and session recordings to understand individual user interactions. 

Advanced data collection methods are A/B testing and prototype evaluations. These controlled experiments allow teams to test variations and measure their impact objectively. This allows extremely accurate predictions about the success of changes. Complementing this quantitative data, teams can also conduct targeted surveys and in-depth user interviews, adding valuable qualitative insights to their decision-making arsenal. 

> Note: Counter-intuitively, successful product teams understand that data is very important but isn't the only factor in decision making. They strike a delicate balance between quantitative data-driven insights, qualitative insights and product intuition developed through experience. This balanced approach helps in interpreting data in context, identifying hidden opportunities, and ensuring that decisions align with the overall product vision and strategy. As we are ambassadors for a data-driven mindset we foster analysis-driven decisions and make sure to be role models  by "drinking our own champagne". 

One of the key benefits of data-driven decision making is the enhanced transparency it provides when teams clearly document the data, analysis, and reasoning behind each significant decision. This documentation can serve as a **valuable tool for communicating decisions to stakeholders across the organisation** and tracking the impact of these decisions against predicted outcomes. It also facilitates a culture of continuous learning and iteration, where the results of data-driven decisions inform future strategies and improve the decision-making process itself. 

Despite its numerous benefits, data-driven decision making is **not without challenges**. First, collecting and analysing data takes a lot of time and expertise. Teams must grapple with issues of data quality, avoid analysis paralysis, and navigate the ethical considerations of data collection and analysis. They must also be adept at recognizing and mitigating potential biases in both data collection and interpretation. Furthermore, as products and markets evolve, teams need to be prepared to adapt their data frameworks accordingly. Finally, even good data does not guarantee a positive outcome, there will always be a certain amount of unpredictability involved in decision making. 

For product teams, fostering a data-driven culture should be a priority. This involves equiping the team with necessary skills and tools, encouraging a spirit of curiosity where assumptions are questioned and data-backed answers are sought, and creating an environment where data-driven wins are celebrated, and failures are viewed as learning opportunities. Data-driven decision making in product management is not just about using data, but about creating a culture where decisions are transparent, accountable, and continuously improving.

# Growth Mindset and Dealing with Non-Success
In product development, not every effort yields the desired outcome, regardless of a team's dedication, user-centricity, or data skills. When a feature fails to meet expectations, dealing with it in a constructive way is referred to as a growth mindset. This principle emphasises learning and improvement over blame and dwelling on past mistakes. 

To apply a growth mindset effectively, teams can follow a structured approach: 
1. **Acknowledge the Situation:** The team collectively recognizes that the expected outcome wasn't achieved, documenting the disparity between expectations and results. 
2. **Form Hypotheses:** The team develops theories about why the feature underperformed, considering factors like usability issues or misalignment with user needs. 
3. **Experimentation (optional):** The team might run A/B tests or release small iterations to test their hypotheses and see if minor changes can improve the feature's performance. 
4. **Decision Making:** Based on the results of their investigation and experiments, the team needs to decide. One of three options must be chosen: 
5. **Iteration (optional):** If they believe the core idea is sound but the execution needs work, they might redesign or refine the feature. 
6. **Pivoting or Deprecation:** If they discover that users have a different but related need, they might pivot the feature to address this newly understood requirement. If the feature truly doesn't add value and can't be salvaged, they should decide to remove it entirely. 
8. **Document Learnings:** Regardless of the decision, the team records insights to inform future product decisions and prevent similar missteps. 
9. **Communicate Transparently:** Share findings and decisions with stakeholders, including users when relevant, to build trust and demonstrate commitment to value delivery. 

Throughout this process, objectivity is key. Teams should focus on data and user needs rather than becoming attached to initial ideas. "Kill your darlings" is a good mantra to follow. It's natural to feel disappointed when outcomes aren't met, especially when there's a strong sense of ownership. Creating space for team members to voice and process this disappointment can be healthy, followed by a shift towards growth and viewing the experience as a valuable learning opportunity. 

By embracing a growth mindset, product teams can turn setbacks into stepping stones for long-term product improvement and success.

# Continuous Learning 
Continuous learning is a cornerstone principle in product management, vital for staying competitive and delivering value in an ever-changing technical and customer landscape. Its importance cannot be overstated, as it enables teams to adapt, innovate, and improve consistently.

At its core, continuous learning in product management acknowledges that no one has all the answers upfront. Business needs evolve, user needs shift, and technologies advance rapidly. By embracing a culture of ongoing learning, product teams position themselves to anticipate and respond to these changes effectively.

One of the primary benefits of continuous learning is its ability to foster innovation. As teams constantly acquire new knowledge and skills, they're better equipped to generate fresh ideas and novel solutions to user problems. This learning-driven innovation can be a key differentiator in crowded markets. 

Moreover, continuous learning helps mitigate risks. By regularly updating their understanding of user needs, market trends, and technological capabilities, product teams can make more informed decisions and avoid costly missteps. It allows them to fail fast, learn quickly, and pivot when necessary. 

To implement continuous learning effectively, teams can adopt (and combine) several strategies: 

- Firstly, they should cultivate a culture that values **curiosity and experimentation**. This means encouraging team members to ask questions, challenge assumptions, and explore new ideas without fear of rejection. Leaders play a significant role here by modelling this behaviour and creating psychological safety within the team. 
- Regular **retrospectives** are another powerful tool. These sessions, held after each sprint or project milestone, allow the team to reflect on what worked well, what didn't, and why. It focuses on team collaboration and team dynamics. The key is to approach these retrospectives with a growth mindset, focusing on learning rather than blame. 
- **Being data-driven** is also a key aspect of continuous learning. Teams should establish robust systems for collecting and analysing user data, usage trends, and product performance metrics. This quantitative information, combined with qualitative insights from user research, provides a solid foundation for learning and improvement. 
- **Cross-functional knowledge sharing** can significantly enhance learning. This might involve regular 'lunch and learn' sessions, where team members or guest speakers share insights on diverse topics. It could also include rotation initiatives, where team members spend time in different roles to broaden their understanding of the entire product development process. 
- External learning opportunities are equally important. Encouraging team members to attend **conferences, workshops, or online courses** can bring fresh perspectives and innovative knowledge into the team. Additionally, staying connected with the broader product management community through forums, social media, or local meetups can provide valuable insights and networking opportunities. 
- Implementing a systematic approach to **documenting and sharing learnings** is another important aspect. This could involve maintaining a team wiki, creating case studies of past projects, or establishing a mentoring program. The goal is to ensure that knowledge doesn't remain siloed but is accessible to the entire team and future team members. 
- Lastly, it's important to **create feedback loops** that extend beyond the immediate team. This includes regular check-ins with users, stakeholders, and partners. Their perspectives can provide invaluable insights that might not be apparent from within the team. 

By prioritising continuous learning, product teams can stay agile, innovative, and user focused. It enables them to navigate the complexities of product development with confidence, turning challenges into opportunities for growth and improvement. In the fast-paced world of product management, the ability to learn and adapt quickly isn't just an advantage - it's a necessity for long-term success

# Cross-functional collaboration 
Cross-functional collaboration is a critical element in successful product management, particularly in a corporate context as the development and success of a product rarely depend on a single department. Instead, it requires the coordinated efforts of various teams, each bringing unique expertise and perspectives to the table. This is where cross-functional collaboration becomes crucial. 

At its essence, cross-functional collaboration in product management involves breaking down silos between different departments and fostering an environment where diverse teams work together towards common goals. Cross-functional teams can be highly effective. For major initiatives, forming temporary teams with members from both product and business departments can lead to more holistic solutions and smoother execution. 

Product teams, typically comprising product managers, designers, and developers, are responsible for creating and improving the product. They focus on user needs, technological feasibility, and product vision. Business teams are mostly concerned with market positioning, revenue generation, and overall business objectives - and in a corporate environment they are often users at the same time. 

One of the primary benefits of strong cross-functional collaboration is the alignment of product development with business goals. Product teams and especially product managers can gain a deeper understanding of market dynamics, revenue targets, and strategic objectives from their business counterparts. This insight helps them prioritise features and improvements that not only delight users but also drive business growth. Conversely, business teams benefit from the product team's deep understanding of user needs and technological capabilities. This knowledge can help shape more realistic business strategies and marketing approaches. 

Effective collaboration also leads to more informed decision-making. When diverse perspectives are brought together, decisions are based on a more comprehensive view of the situation. This can help avoid potential pitfalls and identify opportunities that might have been overlooked in a siloed approach. The challenge to look out for is that a broader set of contributors can sometimes slow down discussions and decisions - this needs to be carefully considered. 

Innovation is another area where cross-functional collaboration has a positive impact. The mixing of different viewpoints and expertise often leads to creative solutions and novel ideas. A finance team member might provide insights that spark a new monetization strategy, or a training team's understanding of users could inspire a game-changing feature. 

To foster effective cross-functional collaboration, especially between product and business teams, several strategies can be employed: 
- **Regular cross-functional communication** is essential. This should go beyond status updates to include collaborative problem-solving sessions and strategic discussions. Meetings should be structured to encourage open dialogue and idea sharing. 
- Shared objectives and KPIs (key performance indicators) can significantly boost collaboration. When product and business teams are working towards **common goals**, it naturally encourages cooperation and mutual support. These shared metrics should balance product-centric measures (like user engagement) with business outcomes (such as revenue growth). 
- Encouraging **job rotations or shadowing programs** between product and business teams can build empathy and understanding. A product manager spending time with the sales team, or a marketing specialist joining sprint planning sessions, can lead to valuable insights and stronger relationships. 
- Clear communication channels and **transparent information sharing** are vital. This might involve using collaboration tools that give all teams visibility into product roadmaps, business forecasts, and customer feedback. 
- Leadership plays a vital role in fostering cross-functional collaboration. Executives should **model collaborative behaviour** and create an organisational culture that values and rewards cross-department cooperation. 

It's important to acknowledge that cross-functional collaboration, especially between product and business teams, can face challenges. Different priorities, timelines, and ways of working can lead to friction. Overcoming these challenges requires patience, clear communication, and a willingness to find common ground.

  # 6) Disaster Recovery (DR) Class

  DR class typically refers to "Disaster Recovery class," which is a classification system used to categorize the level of disaster recovery preparedness and capabilities of an organization or system. It outlines how well an organization can respond to and recover from disruptive events, such as natural disasters, cyberattacks, or system failures.

  ## IT Disaster
  An IT disaster is defined as a functional failure or loss of all or main services including a server room or a complete Data Center. # GxP

  GxP stands for "Good Practice" guidelines and regulations in the pharmaceutical industry, encompassing a set of quality guidelines and regulations that ensure products are safe, meet their intended use, and adhere to quality standards. The "x" in GxP can represent various practices, including GDP, GCP, GLP, etc.

  Overall, GxP regulations are essential for maintaining the quality and safety of pharmaceutical products, ensuring compliance with regulatory requirements, and protecting public health.

# Background and Context

  GxP applications refer to software systems and tools that are designed to comply with Good Practice (GxP) guidelines, which are essential in regulated industries such as pharmaceuticals, biotechnology, and medical devices.

  GxP applications are crucial for:
  - Regulatory Compliance: Ensuring that processes and documentation meet the requirements set by regulatory agencies.
  
  - Data Integrity: Maintaining accurate, reliable, and secure data throughout the product lifecycle.
  
  - Documentation and Traceability: Providing a clear audit trail and documentation to support compliance and facilitate inspections.
  
  - Risk Management: Identifying and mitigating risks associated with product quality, data integrity and patient safety.
  
  - Quality Assurance: Supporting quality management systems to ensure that products are safe and effective.

  Infrastructure qualification in a GxP environment refers to the process of ensuring that the physical and IT infrastructure supporting GxP-regulated activities meets the necessary quality and compliance standards.
